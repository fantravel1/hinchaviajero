Liga BetPlay — The Complete Travel Guide to Colombian Football

Twenty teams, four football-mad cities, altitudes from sea level to 2,850 meters, tickets under $25 USD, and some of the most passionate fan cultures on the continent. Everything you need to plan your Colombian football adventure.

Overview — Colombia's First Division

The Liga BetPlay Dimayor is Colombia's top-flight professional football league, and it is one of the most underrated footballing experiences in all of South America. While Argentine, Brazilian, and Mexican leagues tend to dominate international headlines, Colombian football offers something uniquely its own: a league spread across radically different climates and altitudes, fan cultures that rival anything on the continent, ticket prices that make attending matches genuinely accessible, and a country that has transformed itself into one of the most welcoming destinations for visitors in the region.

The league features 20 teams competing across Colombia's diverse geography. From the Caribbean heat of Barranquilla and Santa Marta at sea level, through the coffee-region cities of Pereira and Manizales in the Andean foothills, up to the high-altitude capital Bogota at 2,640 meters and Tunja at 2,850 meters — a single Liga BetPlay season takes you through an astonishing range of landscapes, climates, and cultures. No other league in Latin America offers this kind of geographic variety within a single country.

Colombian football has produced some of the continent's greatest players — Carlos Valderrama, Rene Higuita, Faustino Asprilla, James Rodriguez, Radamel Falcao, Juan Cuadrado — and the domestic league remains the foundation of that talent pipeline. Clubs like Atletico Nacional, Millonarios, America de Cali, Deportivo Cali, Junior de Barranquilla, and Independiente Santa Fe carry decades of history, rivalries that run generationally deep, and fan bases that fill stadiums with an intensity that has to be experienced firsthand.

For the traveling fan — whether you are a Colombian living abroad returning to see your team, a South American fan following your club in a continental competition, or an international visitor looking for an authentic and affordable football experience — the Liga BetPlay delivers. This guide covers everything you need to plan your trip.

Format — Apertura, Clausura, and the Cuadrangulares

The Liga BetPlay follows an Apertura/Clausura format, which means the season is split into two independent tournaments within a single calendar year. Each tournament produces its own champion, meaning Colombia crowns two league champions per year. This is a common format across Latin American football, and it creates a rhythm of competition that ensures intensity from the first matchday to the last.

Apertura (January - June): The first-half tournament runs from late January or early February through June. All 20 teams play a round-robin of 19 matches (each team faces every other team once). The top eight teams at the end of the round-robin advance to the cuadrangulares — the playoff semifinals that are unique to Colombian football.

Clausura (July - December): The second-half tournament mirrors the Apertura. Another 19-match round-robin from July through November, followed by cuadrangulares and a final in December. The Clausura champion earns the second league title of the year.

The Cuadrangulares: This is where Colombian football gets truly special. The eight qualifying teams are divided into two groups of four, and each group plays a double round-robin (six matches: three home, three away). The winner of each group advances to a two-leg grand final. The cuadrangulares are the peak of the Liga BetPlay season — the matches are fiercely contested, the stadiums are packed, and the atmosphere reaches a level of intensity that few regular-season matches can match. If you can time your trip to coincide with the cuadrangulares in June or December, do it. You will not regret it.

The Grand Final: The two cuadrangulares winners face each other in a two-leg final (home and away) to determine the champion. The final is the single biggest event in Colombian club football, and tickets sell out rapidly. Finals between traditional rivals — Nacional vs. Millonarios, America vs. Cali, Nacional vs. Junior — generate national-level excitement.

HinchaViajero Planning Tip

The cuadrangulares (playoff stage) typically run for three weeks in June and three weeks in December. These are the best weeks to visit Colombia for football. The combination of high stakes, packed stadiums, and elimination pressure creates an atmosphere that the regular season rarely matches. Check the Dimayor website for exact cuadrangulares dates once the round-robin finishes.

Away-Day Logistics — Flights, Buses, and Altitude

Traveling between Liga BetPlay cities involves navigating one of the most geographically diverse countries on earth. Colombia stretches from Caribbean beaches to Andean peaks to Pacific rainforest, and the league's 20 teams are scattered across this entire range. This makes away-day logistics both exciting and demanding — you need to plan for altitude, climate shifts, and varying transport options.

Domestic Flights

Colombia has excellent domestic air connectivity. The main carriers — Avianca, LATAM Colombia, Viva (now part of Avianca), and Wingo — operate frequent flights between all major cities. Bogota's El Dorado International Airport (BOG) serves as the primary hub, with direct flights to Medellin, Cali, Barranquilla, Cartagena, Bucaramanga, Pereira, and most other Liga BetPlay cities.

Altitude — The Invisible Challenge

Altitude is the single most important physical factor to consider when planning a Liga BetPlay trip. Colombia's teams play at elevations ranging from sea level (0 meters) to 2,850 meters, and the physiological impact of moving between these extremes is real. It affects not just players on the pitch but fans in the stands — especially if you are singing, jumping, and exerting yourself in a packed stadium at high altitude.

Altitude Warning

If you are arriving from sea level or a low-altitude city (Barranquilla, Cali, or anywhere abroad below 500m) and heading to Bogota (2,640m) or Tunja (2,850m), arrive at least one full day before the match to acclimatize. Symptoms of altitude sickness include headaches, nausea, shortness of breath, dizziness, and fatigue. Drink plenty of water, avoid heavy meals and alcohol on your first evening, and take it easy physically. Coca tea (agua de coca) and acetaminophen can help with mild symptoms. If you feel severely unwell, descend to a lower altitude and seek medical attention.

Tickets — How to Get In

Tickets for Liga BetPlay matches are among the most affordable in South American football, which is one of the league's greatest advantages for visiting fans. The pricing, availability, and purchasing process are straightforward compared to the complexities of Argentine or Brazilian ticketing systems.

Price ranges: Regular season tickets typically cost between $8 and $25 USD depending on the section and the match. General admission (popular/lateral) sections start as low as 20,000-40,000 COP ($5-10 USD), while better seats in the oriental or occidental stands run 50,000-100,000 COP ($12-25 USD). Cuadrangulares and finals are more expensive, with prices rising to $15-40 USD, but even the most expensive tickets remain a fraction of what you would pay in Argentina, Brazil, or Mexico.

Match Type Popular / General Lateral / Mid-Tier VIP / Preferencial
Regular season (low-profile) $5-8 USD $10-15 USD $15-25 USD
Regular season (derby/big match) $8-12 USD $15-22 USD $22-35 USD
Cuadrangulares (playoffs) $10-15 USD $18-28 USD $28-45 USD
Grand final $15-22 USD $25-40 USD $40-70 USD

Where to buy:

  • Stadium box office (taquilla): For most regular-season matches, you can buy tickets directly at the stadium on matchday. Box offices typically open 3-4 hours before kick-off. This is the simplest and cheapest method — no service fees, no registration required. Bring your passport or cedula (ID is required for all ticket purchases in Colombia).
  • TuBoleta.com: The largest online ticketing platform in Colombia. Most Liga BetPlay clubs use TuBoleta for advance online sales. You will need to create an account and enter your passport or cedula number. Service fees typically add 10-15% to the face value.
  • PrimaBoleta.com: A second authorized online platform used by some clubs. Functions similarly to TuBoleta.
  • Club websites: Some clubs sell directly through their own websites or apps. Atletico Nacional, Millonarios, and Junior all have digital ticket options.

For big matches and derbies: Do not rely on matchday box office sales. Derbies like Millonarios vs. Santa Fe (Clasico Capitalino), Nacional vs. Medellin (Clasico Paisa), or America vs. Cali (Clasico Vallecaucano) sell out in advance. Buy online through TuBoleta 3-7 days before the match. Cuadrangulares and final matches also sell out early — purchase the day tickets go on sale.

Never Buy From Scalpers

Street scalpers (revendedores) operate outside every major stadium in Colombia. Their tickets may be counterfeit, duplicated, or for the wrong section. The risk of being turned away at the gate — or worse, ending up in the wrong section surrounded by hostile fans — is not worth the few dollars you save. Always use official channels. The Great Colombian Derbies

Colombian football is defined by its derbies. These are the matches that fill stadiums beyond capacity, that dominate national conversation for days before and after, and that create the most intense atmospheres in the league. If you are planning a Liga BetPlay trip and want to witness the league at its absolute peak, time your visit to coincide with one of these derbies.

El Clasico Capitalino — Millonarios vs. Santa Fe

Bogota's derby is one of the oldest and most heated rivalries in Colombian football. Millonarios and Independiente Santa Fe have been battling for the soul of the capital since the late 1940s. Millonarios — the "Blue Ballet" — carries the legacy of the El Dorado era and the greatest team in Colombian football history (the team of Alfredo Di Stefano). Santa Fe — the "Cardinal Lion" — wears the pride of being the first Colombian professional football champion. The rivalry is fierce, deeply personal, and runs through families and neighborhoods across Bogota.

The Clasico Capitalino is played at El Campin (officially Estadio Nemesio Camacho), a 36,000-capacity ground in the heart of Bogota that becomes a cauldron on derby night. Visiting fans are typically accommodated in a separate section, but access and allocation depend on the security arrangements for each specific match. Buy tickets early — this derby sells out consistently.

El Clasico Paisa — Atletico Nacional vs. Independiente Medellin

The Medellin derby is arguably the most atmospherically intense match in all of Colombian football. Atletico Nacional — the "Green King of Cups," the only Colombian club to have won the Copa Libertadores (twice) — faces Independiente Medellin — the "Powerful of the Mountain" — in a rivalry that splits the city of eternal spring down the middle.

Both teams play at the Estadio Atanasio Girardot, a 40,000-capacity stadium in the Laureles neighborhood of Medellin. When the Clasico Paisa is on, the entire city revolves around it. Nacional's barra (organized fan group), Los del Sur, is one of the largest and most organized in all of South America. The tifo displays, the coordinated singing, and the sheer volume of noise they produce are world-class. The Medellin derby is a bucket-list match for any football traveler.

El Clasico Vallecaucano — America de Cali vs. Deportivo Cali

Cali's derby pits two of the biggest clubs in Colombian football history against each other. America de Cali — the "Red Devils," famous for their agonizing three consecutive Copa Libertadores final losses in the 1980s — faces Deportivo Cali — the "Sugar Mill," nine-time league champions. The rivalry is old, deep, and loaded with class and neighborhood associations that give it a social dimension beyond sport.

America plays at the Estadio Pascual Guerrero (capacity 35,000), while Deportivo Cali uses the Estadio Deportivo Cali (capacity 52,000) in the southern suburb of Palmira. When they meet in the Clasico Vallecaucano, the host stadium is determined by who has home advantage. The atmosphere is electric, the city splits cleanly in two, and the salsa music that soundtracks Cali's nightlife becomes the backdrop to pre-match celebrations.

Other Key Rivalries

  • Junior vs. Union Magdalena (Clasico Costeno): The Caribbean coast derby between Barranquilla and Santa Marta. Played in searing heat with cumbia and vallenato setting the mood. Junior's Estadio Metropolitano (capacity 46,000) is one of the largest in the country.
  • Nacional vs. Millonarios: Not a local derby but arguably the biggest club fixture in Colombia. The two most successful clubs in the country, from the two most important cities. When they meet, the nation watches.
  • Once Caldas vs. Deportivo Pereira (Clasico Cafetero): The coffee-region derby between Manizales and Pereira. Smaller in scale but deeply felt in the Eje Cafetero. Great for fans who want a more intimate Colombian football experience.
  • Deportes Tolima vs. Atletico Huila: The Tolima-Huila derby from central Colombia. Ibague vs. Neiva, hot weather, and passionate smaller-city fan bases.

Money tips:

  • Currency: Colombian Peso (COP). As of early 2026, approximately 4,000-4,200 COP = 1 USD. ATMs are widely available. Withdraw cash in reasonable amounts — many street vendors and smaller taquillas only accept cash.
  • Street food is your friend: Empanadas (1,000-2,000 COP / $0.25-0.50 USD), arepas con queso (2,000-4,000 COP / $0.50-1 USD), bandeja paisa (15,000-25,000 COP / $4-6 USD at a local restaurant). You can eat very well for very little.
  • Beer: A Club Colombia, Poker, or Aguila beer at a tienda (corner store) costs about 3,000-5,000 COP ($0.75-1.25 USD). Inside the stadium, beers (when available) cost 5,000-10,000 COP ($1.25-2.50 USD). Some stadiums prohibit alcohol sales for certain matches.
  • Ride-hailing: Use InDriver, Uber, or DiDi for city transport. Rides within major cities rarely exceed 15,000-25,000 COP ($4-6 USD). Much safer and more reliable than street taxis.
Budget Hack

For the ultimate budget Liga BetPlay weekend, take a Friday night bus to the away city (saving a night's accommodation), arrive Saturday morning, explore the city during the day, attend the match in the evening, sleep at a hostel Saturday night, and take a Sunday morning bus back. Total cost for a Bogota-Medellin away-day on this plan: approximately $50-70 USD all-in. That is arguably the best value-for-money football travel experience in South America.

Safety — Improving, But Stay Smart

Colombia's reputation for safety has transformed dramatically over the past two decades. The Colombia of 2026 is a fundamentally different country from the Colombia of the 1990s, and millions of international visitors travel safely through the country every year. That said, like any Latin American country, it requires sensible precautions — especially when attending football matches, which can concentrate large crowds and heightened emotions in one place.

General safety for football travelers:

  • Travel in groups. Whether you are with friends or connecting with fellow fans, avoid being alone in unfamiliar areas near stadiums before and after matches.
  • Use ride-hailing apps. InDriver, Uber, and DiDi are widely available and much safer than hailing taxis on the street. This is especially important when leaving the stadium after a match, when large crowds disperse in all directions and pickpockets may be active.
  • Do not flash valuables. Leave expensive watches, jewelry, and cameras at your accommodation. Take only your phone and the cash you need for the day. Keep your phone in a front pocket or a secure crossbody bag.
  • Avoid wearing visiting team colors outside the stadium. If you are attending as a visiting fan, keep your jersey in your bag until you reach the designated visiting section. Wearing visiting colors in the streets around the stadium — particularly for derbies — is a provocation that can lead to trouble.
  • Know the stadium neighborhoods. Some stadium areas in Colombian cities are fine during the day but become less safe at night. Research the area around the stadium before your visit and plan your arrival and departure routes. Our city guides for Bogota, Medellin, and Barranquilla include matchday neighborhood breakdowns.
  • Follow police instructions. Colombian police (Policia Nacional) deploy heavily around stadiums on matchdays. Follow their directions for entering and leaving the stadium. In high-tension matches, police may restrict movement around certain areas — cooperate, even if it is inconvenient.
  • Be aware of the barra dynamic. Colombian barras (organized fan groups) are passionate and can be territorial. They occupy the popular (general admission) sections behind the goals. If you want to experience the atmosphere from within a barra, understand that it is intense — standing throughout, constant singing, jumping, and crowd surges. If that sounds overwhelming, buy a seat in the lateral or preferencial sections where the experience is more relaxed.
Derby Matches — Extra Precautions

For high-tension derbies (Clasico Capitalino, Clasico Paisa, Clasico Vallecaucano), security is significantly heightened. Police may restrict visiting fan access to specific entry points and designated routes. Arrive early — gates for big matches can involve long queues and thorough searches. Avoid alcohol-fueled confrontations near the stadium. If you sense trouble, move away and seek a police officer. Colombian authorities take stadium violence seriously and have improved security protocols substantially in recent years.

Key Cities for Liga BetPlay

Four cities dominate the Liga BetPlay landscape. Each offers a completely different football experience, tied to its unique climate, culture, altitude, and fan identity. Here is a quick overview of the essential cities for any Liga BetPlay trip.

Bogota — The Capital at 2,640m

Clubs: Millonarios, Independiente Santa Fe, La Equidad
Stadium: Estadio Nemesio Camacho "El Campin" (36,343 capacity)
Altitude: 2,640m
Climate: Cool and rainy (bring a jacket, 8-19°C year-round)

Bogota is the center of Colombian football. Millonarios and Santa Fe are two of the most successful clubs in the league's history, and their shared home — El Campin — is one of the most iconic stadiums in South America. The stadium sits in the Teusaquillo neighborhood, well-connected by TransMilenio (Bogota's BRT system) and surrounded by restaurants, bars, and pre-match gathering spots. Bogota offers the richest cultural experience alongside the football: world-class museums (Museo del Oro, Museo Botero), the historic La Candelaria neighborhood, and a food scene that ranges from high-end restaurants to incredible street carts. Medellin — The City of Eternal Spring at 1,495m

Clubs: Atletico Nacional, Independiente Medellin
Stadium: Estadio Atanasio Girardot (40,043 capacity)
Altitude: 1,495m
Climate: Mild and spring-like (18-28°C year-round)

Medellin is the football capital of passion. Atletico Nacional has the largest fan base in Colombia and one of the most organized barras on the continent. The Atanasio Girardot is located in the Laureles-Estadio neighborhood, a pleasant area with plenty of restaurants, cafes, and hostels within walking distance. The city itself has undergone a remarkable urban transformation and is one of the most visited cities in South America. Beyond football, explore the Comuna 13 graffiti tour, ride the metrocable over the city, and enjoy the Poblado nightlife. Medellin's climate — perpetual spring — makes it the most comfortable city for matchday attendance. Read our full Medellin guide.

Cali — The Salsa Capital at 1,000m

Clubs: America de Cali, Deportivo Cali
Stadiums: Estadio Pascual Guerrero (35,000 capacity), Estadio Deportivo Cali (52,000 capacity)
Altitude: 1,000m
Climate: Warm (25-30°C year-round)

Cali is where football meets salsa. The city's two clubs — America and Deportivo Cali — have enormous fan bases, and the rivalry between them defines the social fabric of the city. America's Pascual Guerrero sits in the San Fernando neighborhood, close to the city center, while Deportivo Cali's modern stadium is in Palmira, about 25 minutes south. Cali's warmth (both climatic and cultural) makes it a uniquely enjoyable football destination. Pre-match culture here revolves around salsa music, aguardiente, and street food from the Pacific coast tradition. Do not miss the Clasico Vallecaucano if your dates align.

Barranquilla — The Caribbean at Sea Level

Club: Junior de Barranquilla
Stadium: Estadio Metropolitano Roberto Melendez (46,692 capacity)
Altitude: 0-18m
Climate: Hot and humid (30-35°C year-round)

Barranquilla offers a completely different Liga BetPlay experience. At sea level on the Caribbean coast, the climate is hot and humid — matches played in the evening are still played in 28-30°C heat. Junior de Barranquilla is the dominant club of the coast and plays at the massive Estadio Metropolitano, which also hosts Colombian national team home matches. The fan culture here is Caribbean — louder, more musical, heavily influenced by cumbia and vallenato rhythms. The pre-match experience includes street parties, grilled meat vendors, and an energy that feels more like a carnival than a football match. Bring sunscreen for afternoon matches and stay hydrated. Practical Tips for the Traveling Fan

Here is a collection of practical advice for anyone planning their first (or fifth) Liga BetPlay trip. These tips come from experience on the ground and from the community of fans who travel Colombia regularly for football.

  • Arrive at the stadium early. Gates typically open 2-3 hours before kick-off. For big matches, expect long queues for security screenings. Arriving 90 minutes before kick-off is a good baseline.
  • Bring cash. Many stadium vendors and some box offices only accept cash. ATMs inside stadiums are rare. Withdraw money before heading to the ground.
  • Dress for the climate, not the occasion. If you are attending a match in Barranquilla, shorts and a t-shirt are appropriate. If you are in Bogota or Tunja, bring a jacket — nighttime temperatures can drop to 6-10°C. Rain gear is essential in Bogota, where afternoon showers are common year-round.
  • Learn basic Spanish. English is not widely spoken outside tourist areas and upscale hotels. Knowing basic phrases — "una boleta por favor" (one ticket please), "donde queda la tribuna norte?" (where is the north stand?) — will make your life much easier.
  • Download Rappi or iFood. These delivery apps can be useful for ordering food to your accommodation before or after the match. They also help you discover local restaurants near your hotel.
  • Check the fixture schedule weekly. Liga BetPlay matches are scheduled by Dimayor, and kick-off times can change with relatively short notice — especially for matches broadcast on Win Sports (the primary rights holder). Always confirm the exact time and date 3-5 days before traveling.
  • SIM cards and data. Get a local SIM card (Claro, Movistar, or Tigo) at the airport or any electronics store. Data plans are extremely cheap — approximately $5-10 USD for 5-10 GB. Having mobile data ensures you can use ride-hailing apps, maps, and real-time translation.
  • Respect the barra. If you end up in the popular section (behind the goals) where the organized barras sit, follow their lead. Stand when they stand, sing when they sing (or at least try), and do not sit down in front of them. They take their choreography and participation seriously, and you will have a much better experience if you participate rather than observe.

Most Liga BetPlay tickets can be purchased at the stadium box office (taquilla) on matchday for regular-season games. Arrive 2-3 hours before kick-off with your passport, as ID is required for all ticket purchases. Liga BetPlay venues range from sea level (Barranquilla, Santa Marta) to 2,850 meters (Tunja). If you are traveling from a low-altitude city to Bogota (2,640m) or Tunja (2,850m), arrive at least one day early to acclimatize. Common altitude symptoms include headaches, shortness of breath, dizziness, and fatigue — these are especially noticeable if you are singing and jumping in the stands. Drink plenty of water, avoid heavy alcohol consumption on your first day, eat light meals, and consider coca tea (agua de coca) for mild symptoms. If you feel severely unwell, descend to a lower altitude and seek medical attention.
The Liga BetPlay runs January through December, so there is almost always football to see. The absolute best time is during the cuadrangulares (playoff stages) in June and December — that is when the atmosphere is at its peak. For regular season matches with good weather, February-April and August-October are ideal. Avoid mid-year and end-of-year FIFA international breaks (usually two weeks in March, June, September, and October/November) when the league pauses. Check the Dimayor calendar for exact dates before booking your trip.
Colombia is one of the most affordable football destinations in South America. A budget away-day (overnight bus, hostel, match ticket, street food) costs $35-75 USD for a single-night trip between major cities. Intercity buses cost $10-30 USD for most routes, hostels run $8-15 USD per night, match tickets start at $5-8 USD for popular sections, and street food meals cost $2-4 USD. Even a comfortable mid-range trip with domestic flights and a decent hotel rarely exceeds $150-250 USD for a weekend. The Liga BetPlay offers arguably the best value-for-money football travel experience on the continent.
Colombia offers visa-free entry for citizens of most countries in the Americas, the European Union, the United Kingdom, Australia, and many other nations for stays of up to 90 days. South American nationals from Mercosur and CAN member states can enter with just their national ID card. US, Canadian, and EU passport holders receive a tourist stamp on arrival. Always check the latest entry requirements with the Colombian consulate or on the Migracion Colombia website before traveling, as policies can change. Carry your passport, proof of onward travel, and proof of accommodation.
If you can only attend one derby, go for the Clasico Paisa (Atletico Nacional vs. Independiente Medellin). It offers the most intense atmosphere in Colombian football, played at the 40,000-seat Atanasio Girardot in the beautiful city of Medellin. The Clasico Capitalino (Millonarios vs. Santa Fe) in Bogota is also excellent and easier to access for international travelers since Bogota is the main entry point to Colombia. The Clasico Vallecaucano (America vs. Deportivo Cali) is the most emotionally charged if you like a rivalry with deep social undercurrents, set in the salsa capital of the world.
