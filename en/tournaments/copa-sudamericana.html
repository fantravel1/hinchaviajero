Accessible Tournament

Copa Sudamericana: The Complete Travel Guide

South America's second continental tournament is the best-kept secret in football travel. More affordable, more accessible, and packed with the kind of raw passion that only mid-tier clubs with everything to prove can deliver. This is the away-day that every fan should start with.

Overview: South America's Europa League

If the Copa Libertadores is South America's Champions League, the Copa Sudamericana is its Europa League -- and just like the Europa League, it is massively underrated. Dismissed by casual fans as "the other tournament," the Sudamericana is in fact one of the most rewarding competitions on the continent for the traveling supporter. It is more accessible, more affordable, and often more surprising than its prestigious older sibling.

Organized by CONMEBOL, the Copa Sudamericana brings together 44 clubs from across all ten South American football nations -- Argentina, Bolivia, Brazil, Chile, Colombia, Ecuador, Paraguay, Peru, Uruguay, and Venezuela. The tournament runs annually from March through November, following a format that has evolved significantly since the competition's founding in 2002. Clubs that do not qualify for the Copa Libertadores, or that are eliminated in the Libertadores qualifying rounds, enter the Sudamericana. This means the tournament features a mix of ambitious mid-table clubs, rising regional powers, and occasional fallen giants looking for continental redemption.

What makes the Sudamericana special for traveling fans? Several things. First, the ticket prices are dramatically lower than the Libertadores. Where a Libertadores group stage ticket might cost $20-50 USD, a Sudamericana equivalent can be found for $10-25 USD. Second, the stadiums are smaller, which creates more intimate atmospheres where you are closer to the pitch and closer to the action. Third, the security environment is generally more relaxed -- there is less police presence, fewer barricades, and a less intense confrontation between home and visiting fanbases. And fourth, the cities you visit are often off the traditional tourist radar, giving you the chance to discover places that most travelers never see.

None of this means the Sudamericana lacks passion. On the contrary, for many of the clubs involved, this tournament is the biggest stage they will ever play on. When a club from a small Paraguayan city or a second-tier Argentine suburb reaches the knockout rounds of the Sudamericana, the entire community rallies behind them with a fervor that rivals anything you will see in the Libertadores. These are the nights when 25,000-capacity stadiums feel like they hold 100,000, when every voice in the stand is screaming in unison, and when the line between football and life disappears completely.

"The Libertadores is where the giants clash. The Sudamericana is where the dreamers play. And the dreamers always sing louder." -- Traveling fan from Lanus, 2024 Sudamericana finalist

Format: 44 Clubs, One Dream

The Copa Sudamericana's format has been restructured multiple times since 2002, but the current system -- in place since 2021 -- mirrors the Libertadores structure closely while accommodating a larger pool of clubs. Understanding the format is essential for planning your travel, because the number of matches, the timing of each phase, and the draw itself determine where you will need to go and when.

Phase 1 -- Group Stage (March-May): The 44 clubs are divided into eight groups of four teams each (32 clubs) and a separate preliminary knockout round for the remaining 12. Each group contains teams from different countries, ensuring international travel from the very first matchday. Teams play six matches -- three home and three away -- over approximately eight weeks. The top two from each group advance to the knockout rounds. For the traveling fan, the group stage is the golden window: ticket availability is at its peak, travel costs are standard, and the variety of destinations is enormous.

Phase 2 -- Round of 32 (June): This round is unique to the Sudamericana. The 16 group stage qualifiers are joined by 16 clubs eliminated from the Copa Libertadores group stage, creating a 32-team bracket. Matches are played over two legs (home and away). This injection of Libertadores clubs raises the quality and occasionally the ticket demand, but prices remain manageable. Plan 3-4 weeks ahead for transport and accommodation.

Phase 3 -- Round of 16 (July-August): Sixteen teams remain, still playing two-legged ties. The intensity increases noticeably. Clubs that have survived this far begin to believe they can win the tournament, and their fanbases respond accordingly. Stadiums fill to higher capacities, and away sections become louder. Ticket demand rises, but nothing like the Libertadores equivalent. Plan 4-6 weeks ahead.

Phase 4 -- Quarterfinals and Semifinals (September-October): Eight teams in the quarters, four in the semis. This is where the Sudamericana transforms from a pleasant tournament into a genuine emotional war. The clubs remaining are fighting for a spot in the final and a CONMEBOL continental title -- for many of them, the biggest prize in their entire history. Atmospheres in the semifinals are extraordinary. Plan as soon as the draw is made.

Phase 5 -- The Final (November): Like the Libertadores, the Sudamericana final has moved to a single-match format at a pre-determined neutral venue. The final is the most sought-after ticket in the tournament, but it remains far more accessible than a Libertadores final. CONMEBOL distributes tickets to both finalist clubs, and a portion is sold to the general public through official channels. Prices are reasonable by continental standards, typically ranging from $30-80 USD.

Libertadores Dropout Effect

The Round of 32 is when clubs eliminated from the Copa Libertadores group stage drop into the Sudamericana bracket. This can dramatically raise the profile of the matches -- you might find yourself watching a former Libertadores contender with a massive fanbase playing in a smaller Sudamericana away ground. These mismatches produce some of the most electric atmospheres of the entire tournament, as the smaller club's fans rally to topple a giant on their own turf.

Calendar: When to Plan Your Trip

The Copa Sudamericana runs from March through November, covering most of the South American football calendar. This long season gives you multiple windows to plan a trip, but each phase of the tournament offers a different experience. Here is a month-by-month breakdown of what to expect and when to act.

January-February (Pre-tournament): CONMEBOL confirms participating clubs and publishes the group stage draw. This is the time to check if your team has qualified and start monitoring travel options. If your club is in the Sudamericana, begin setting up price alerts for flights and buses to the cities of your group opponents. No matches are played yet, so prices are at their baseline.

March-May (Group Stage): The best window for first-time travelers. Six matchdays spread over eight weeks mean you have multiple opportunities to catch a match. Weather across South America is transitioning from late summer to autumn (remember, seasons are reversed south of the equator), so temperatures are comfortable in most host cities. Transport and hotel prices are at normal levels. This is when you should make your move if you want the easiest, most affordable Sudamericana experience.

June (Round of 32): South American winter begins. Matches in southern cities (Buenos Aires, Santiago, Montevideo, Curitiba) will be cold -- pack accordingly. The Round of 32 is a quick phase with matches played over two to three weeks. If your team is involved, act fast because the turnaround between the draw and the first leg can be as short as ten days.

July-August (Round of 16): The heart of winter. The tournament is now in full knockout mode, and the remaining clubs are genuinely competitive. This is when the Sudamericana starts generating serious media attention and fan mobilization. Hotels in smaller host cities can start filling up for matchdays, so plan ahead.

September-October (Quarterfinals and Semifinals): Spring arrives in the southern hemisphere, bringing better weather and longer days. These are the most intense months of the tournament. If your team makes the semifinals, expect an emotional rollercoaster. Transport prices may increase for popular routes, especially if two clubs from the same country are drawn together.

November (The Final): The single-match final is held at a neutral venue announced months in advance. Late spring means warm weather across most of the continent. The final weekend becomes a mini-festival, with both fanbases arriving in the host city days before the match. Book everything the moment the venue is announced.

Schedule Changes Are Common

CONMEBOL frequently reschedules Sudamericana matches, sometimes with as little as one week's notice. Television broadcasting requirements, security concerns, and domestic league conflicts can all cause date changes. Never book non-refundable transport or accommodation until the match date is officially confirmed. Set up alerts on the CONMEBOL Sudamericana official website and follow your club's social media for real-time schedule updates. Flexibility is your best friend in this tournament.

Away-Day: Ideal for First-Timers

If you have never attended an international football match abroad, the Copa Sudamericana is where you should start. Not the Libertadores, not a World Cup qualifier -- the Sudamericana. Here is why, and how to make the most of your first continental away-day.

Lower pressure, same passion: The Sudamericana does not carry the win-or-die intensity of the Libertadores, which means the overall atmosphere is more welcoming. Home fans are less hostile toward visitors, police presence is proportional rather than overwhelming, and the emotional temperature is high without being dangerous. You can enjoy the experience without constantly looking over your shoulder.

Smaller stadiums, better experience: Many Sudamericana matches are played in stadiums with capacities between 15,000 and 35,000. This is a sweet spot for the traveling fan: you are close enough to the pitch to see facial expressions, close enough to the opposing fans to feel their energy, and the acoustics of a smaller, packed stadium amplify every chant. Compare this to a 60,000-seat Libertadores venue where you might be 100 meters from the action in a distant upper tier.

Easier ticket access: Getting a ticket to a Sudamericana match is dramatically easier than for the Libertadores. Group stage matches rarely sell out, and even knockout round tickets are obtainable with reasonable advance planning. You do not necessarily need to be a socio of the club to get access -- many host clubs sell tickets to the general public through their box offices or online platforms. Away allocations are smaller but proportionally easier to obtain because fewer fans travel.

Discover hidden gems: The Sudamericana takes you to cities that the Libertadores rarely visits. Instead of Buenos Aires, Sao Paulo, and Bogota, you might find yourself in Florianopolis, Barinas, Ibague, Oruro, or Ciudad del Este. These are not tourist destinations -- they are real, unfiltered South American cities where your presence as a foreign football fan will be noticed, appreciated, and often celebrated. The locals will want to talk to you, feed you, and tell you everything about their club. These interactions are the soul of football travel, and they happen more naturally in the Sudamericana than in any other tournament.

Your First Sudamericana Away-Day: Step by Step

  1. Pick a group stage match in a neighboring country with easy transport connections. Short distances and straightforward border crossings reduce the variables on your first trip.
  2. Buy your ticket early. Check the host club's official website or social media for ticket sale announcements. For group stage matches, tickets are often available at the box office on matchday, but do not rely on this -- buy in advance if possible.
  3. Book refundable transport. An overnight bus is the classic first-timer approach: affordable, atmospheric, and it connects you with other traveling fans on the same route.
  4. Pack light. A small backpack with essentials. Leave valuables at home. Carry only the cash you need for the day, plus your phone, ID, and ticket.
  5. Arrive early at the stadium. Soak in the pre-match atmosphere outside the ground. Find the food stalls, listen to the chants, feel the energy building. This is as much a part of the experience as the match itself.
  6. Follow the crowd after the match. Do not wander off alone in an unfamiliar city at night. Stay with the group of traveling fans, take organized transport back to the city center, and celebrate responsibly.
First-Timer Recommendation

If you are based in or traveling through Buenos Aires, one of the best first Sudamericana away-days is a trip to Asuncion, Paraguay or Montevideo, Uruguay. Both cities are reachable by overnight bus (16-18 hours to Asuncion, 8-10 hours to Montevideo), have friendly and safe stadium environments, and offer affordable everything -- food, accommodation, tickets. The fans in Paraguay and Uruguay are welcoming to visitors, and the stadiums are compact enough to deliver an unforgettable atmosphere. For your first-ever international match abroad, you cannot do better.

Tickets: $10-80 USD

One of the Copa Sudamericana's greatest advantages over the Libertadores is its ticket pricing. This is a tournament where you can attend a continental match for less than the price of a meal at a mid-range restaurant. The affordability is real, consistent, and one of the main reasons we recommend the Sudamericana as the entry point for international football travel.

How pricing works: Ticket prices for Sudamericana matches are set by the home club, not by CONMEBOL (except for the final). This means prices vary significantly between countries and clubs. A group stage match at a small Bolivian or Venezuelan club might cost as little as $5-10 USD for general admission, while the same round at a larger Argentine or Brazilian club could be $15-30 USD. The key insight is that prices across the Sudamericana are universally lower than the Libertadores equivalent, often by 40-60%.

Where to buy: For home section tickets, check the host club's official website, their social media channels, or visit the physical box office (boleteria) on matchday. Many South American clubs still rely heavily on physical box office sales, especially for smaller competitions like the Sudamericana. For away section tickets, contact your own club's ticket office or member services. Unlike the Libertadores, where away allocations sell out instantly, Sudamericana away tickets are often available up to a few days before the match during the group stage.

Can I buy at the gate? For group stage matches at smaller clubs, yes -- tickets are frequently available at the box office on matchday. This is not guaranteed, but it is common. For knockout rounds, do not count on gate sales. Always try to secure your ticket in advance, even if the match is unlikely to sell out. Arriving at a stadium in a foreign country without a ticket is a stress you do not need on your first away-day.

Even at Low Prices, Scams Exist

The lower profile of the Sudamericana does not make it immune to ticket scams. Street scalpers outside stadiums, fake tickets on social media, and duplicate QR codes all exist in this tournament too. The same rules apply: only buy from official club channels, authorized platforms, or the physical box office. Never buy from strangers on WhatsApp, Facebook groups, or outside the stadium. If someone approaches you near the ground offering a ticket at face value or below, walk away. Read our full Tickets Without Scams guide for comprehensive protection strategies.

Best Atmospheres: Mid-Tier Clubs with Giant Hearts

The Copa Sudamericana's greatest charm is the clubs themselves. These are not the Boca Juniors and Flamengos of the continent (though some of those occasionally appear after dropping from the Libertadores). These are the mid-tier clubs -- the clubs that live and die by this tournament, for whom a Sudamericana semifinal is the biggest night in a generation. And their fans deliver atmospheres that punch far above their weight.

Independiente del Valle (Ecuador): Based in Sangolqui, a small city in the Quito valley, Independiente del Valle has become one of the most successful Sudamericana clubs in recent years, winning the tournament in 2019. Their modest Estadio Banco Guayaquil (capacity 12,000) becomes a cauldron on match nights. The fan culture is young, energetic, and deeply connected to the community. Attending a match here feels like watching a revolution unfold in real time.

Defensa y Justicia (Argentina): From Florencio Varela, a working-class suburb south of Buenos Aires, Defensa y Justicia won the Sudamericana in 2020. Their Estadio Norberto Tomaghello (capacity 16,000) is a no-frills ground where the fans are packed tight against the pitch. The atmosphere is raw, loud, and authentic -- this is not a polished stadium experience; it is football in its most stripped-down, passionate form.

Athletico Paranaense (Brazil): Based in Curitiba, Athletico (not to be confused with Atletico Mineiro) plays at the Arena da Baixada (capacity 42,000), one of the most modern stadiums in South America. Despite the larger venue, the fans -- known as the Furacão (Hurricane) -- create walls of sound that fill every corner. They won the Sudamericana in 2018 and 2021, and their European-quality stadium combined with Brazilian intensity makes this one of the best grounds to visit on the continent.

Liga de Quito (Ecuador): Playing at altitude in Quito (2,850 meters), Liga's Estadio Rodrigo Paz Delgado (capacity 41,000) offers a unique challenge for visiting teams and fans alike. The thin air amplifies fatigue, the cold night temperatures add an edge to the atmosphere, and Liga's supporters bring flags, drums, and an intensity that takes advantage of every meter of altitude. The 2009 Sudamericana champions remain a force in the competition.

Lanus (Argentina): Another Buenos Aires suburb club with a deeply loyal fanbase. Their Estadio Ciudad de Lanus (capacity 47,000) is among the largest in the Sudamericana, and when it fills for a knockout match, the noise is staggering. Lanus won the Sudamericana in 2013 and have consistently been competitive in the tournament. The surrounding neighborhood of Lanus is a classic Argentine barrio -- expect asado smoke, corner kioscos, and football talk at every sidewalk cafe.

Deportivo Cali (Colombia): Based in Cali, known as the "capital de la salsa," Deportivo Cali's Estadio Deportivo Cali (capacity 52,000) combines Colombian football passion with the rhythmic energy of the city itself. Sudamericana nights at this ground feature choreographed tifo displays, non-stop singing from the barra, and an atmosphere that reflects Cali's reputation as one of the most joyful cities on the continent.

Clubs from Paraguay and Uruguay: Do not overlook the smaller clubs from these two countries. Sol de America, Sportivo Luqueno, and General Caballero in Paraguay, and Liverpool FC Montevideo, Danubio, and Defensor Sporting in Uruguay, all have devoted supporters who transform their compact stadiums into pressure cookers during Sudamericana matches. These grounds, typically holding between 8,000 and 20,000, offer the most intimate continental football experience available.

Budget: More Affordable Than You Think

The Copa Sudamericana is the most budget-friendly continental tournament in South American football. This is not a marketing claim -- it is a reflection of lower ticket prices, smaller host cities with cheaper accommodation, and the general economics of mid-tier football. If money has ever stopped you from planning an international football trip, the Sudamericana removes that barrier.

Below are realistic per-person budgets for different travel styles across the tournament's phases. All figures include transport, ticket, accommodation, food, and miscellaneous expenses.

Budget tier: Overnight buses, hostels or fan houses, street food and market meals, cheapest available ticket. This is how university students and young fans travel, and it is entirely viable. The overnight bus is the cornerstone -- it eliminates one night of accommodation and costs a fraction of a flight. A typical budget group stage trip: $25-40 USD bus ticket, $10-15 USD ticket, $10-20 USD hostel, $15-25 USD food. Total: $60-100 USD for a neighboring country trip, without including any extras.

Mid-range tier: Low-cost flights (JetSMART, SKY, Gol, Viva Air), a clean hotel or good Airbnb, local restaurants, and mid-tier ticket. This is the sweet spot for working adults who want comfort without luxury. The biggest expense at this tier is the flight, which can be managed by booking 3-5 weeks early and using fare alerts.

Comfortable tier: Full-service flights, a hotel in a central or stadium-adjacent location, dining at well-reviewed restaurants, and the best available ticket. This tier is common for families, groups of older fans, and travelers from outside South America. Even at this level, the Sudamericana is dramatically cheaper than an equivalent trip to a European continental match.

Budget Hack: Combine Two Matches

If your team plays two group stage away matches within a few weeks of each other in the same region, consider staying in the area between matches instead of returning home and traveling out again. For example, if your Argentine club has away matches in Asuncion and then in Curitiba two weeks later, you could spend those two weeks traveling through Paraguay and southern Brazil on a backpacker budget, attending both matches for roughly the same total transport cost as a single round trip. This turns a football trip into a proper adventure.

Safety for Traveling Fans

Safety is a legitimate concern for any traveler in South America, and football matches add a layer of complexity. The good news is that the Copa Sudamericana, by its nature, is generally safer for visiting fans than the Copa Libertadores. The lower stakes, the smaller crowds, and the less intense rivalries all contribute to a more manageable security environment. That said, preparation is still essential.

Before you travel:

  • Research the host city. Read our Matchday Safety guide for city-specific tips. Identify which neighborhoods around the stadium are safe and which to avoid.
  • Share your travel itinerary with someone you trust. Use a live location-sharing app (WhatsApp, Google Maps) so someone always knows where you are.
  • Register your trip with your country's embassy or consulate in the host country, especially if you are traveling from outside South America.
  • Purchase travel insurance that covers medical emergencies, theft, and trip cancellation. This is non-negotiable for any international football trip.

On matchday:

  • Keep your team's jersey in your bag until you are inside or immediately adjacent to the stadium. Do not wear club colors while navigating unfamiliar city streets.
  • Travel to and from the stadium in groups. Never walk to the ground alone, especially at night.
  • Carry only the cash you need for the day. Leave cards, extra cash, passport (carry a photocopy instead), and valuables at your accommodation.
  • Use ride-hailing apps (Uber, Cabify, DiDi) instead of street taxis. Screen-grab your route and driver details.
  • Follow police instructions without argument. In knockout rounds, police may designate specific routes and gathering points for visiting fans -- use them.
  • Have a clear exit plan before entering the stadium. Know which gate you entered through, which direction leads to your transport, and have an alternative route in case of congestion or incidents.

After the match:

  • Leave the stadium area promptly but calmly. Do not linger outside the ground in unfamiliar territory.
  • If your team lost, manage your emotions. Frustration is natural, but expressing it loudly in a host city's streets can attract unwanted attention.
  • Head directly to your accommodation or transport. Avoid bars or gathering spots in the immediate stadium neighborhood if you do not know the area well.
  • Text or call your safety contact to confirm you are safe after the match.
Critical Safety Rule

Never enter the home section wearing or carrying visiting team colors. In the Sudamericana, this is less dangerous than in the Libertadores, but it is still a serious risk. If you have purchased a home section ticket because away allocations were unavailable, you must behave as a neutral throughout the entire match -- no celebrating goals, no singing your team's songs, no visible club merchandise. If you cannot commit to this discipline, do not buy a home section ticket. Your safety depends on your ability to blend in completely.

History: From 2002 to Today

The Copa Sudamericana was born in 2002, making it a young tournament by continental standards. Its creation filled a gap in the South American football calendar: a second-tier continental competition that gave more clubs the opportunity to compete internationally. Over its two-plus decades of existence, the Sudamericana has evolved from a forgettable consolation prize into a genuinely prestigious tournament with its own identity, traditions, and legendary moments.

2002: The Inaugural Edition. The first Copa Sudamericana was won by San Lorenzo de Almagro of Argentina, who defeated Atletico Nacional of Colombia in the final. The tournament immediately established itself as a serious competition -- San Lorenzo treated it as a major title, and their celebrations set the tone for how clubs would approach the Sudamericana going forward.

2003-2010: Finding Its Identity. The early years saw the tournament experiment with different formats, including a system where Copa Libertadores losers entered the Sudamericana bracket mid-tournament. Champions from this era include Cienciano of Peru (2003) -- one of the most romantic victories in South American football history, as a club from Cusco at 3,400 meters altitude defeated River Plate in the final -- and Arsenal de Sarandi (2007), a tiny club from suburban Buenos Aires that shocked the continent. These underdog stories became the Sudamericana's signature.

2011-2016: Growing Prestige. As CONMEBOL invested more in the tournament's organization, broadcasting, and prize money, the Sudamericana began attracting stronger clubs and larger audiences. Universidad de Chile (2011), Sao Paulo (2012), and Lanus (2013) all treated their Sudamericana campaigns as serious priorities, not consolation runs. The quality of football improved, and so did the fan mobilization for knockout rounds and finals.

2017: The Chapecoense Tragedy. The 2016 Sudamericana final was marred by unspeakable tragedy when the plane carrying Chapecoense of Brazil crashed in Colombia en route to the first leg of the final against Atletico Nacional. Seventy-one of the 77 people on board perished, including most of the playing squad. Atletico Nacional asked CONMEBOL to award the title to Chapecoense, a gesture of extraordinary sportsmanship. The tragedy deeply affected the tournament's identity and led to significant improvements in travel safety protocols for participating clubs.

2018-Present: The Modern Era. The tournament was restructured in 2017 to mirror the Libertadores format more closely, with a proper group stage introduced and a single-match final format adopted. Recent champions include Athletico Paranaense (2018, 2021), Independiente del Valle (2019), Defensa y Justicia (2020), and Independiente del Valle again (2022). The emergence of repeat champions from smaller clubs has given the Sudamericana a narrative of meritocracy -- this is a tournament where any well-run club, regardless of size, can win continental glory.

Sudamericana vs. Libertadores: A Travel Comparison

If you are deciding between planning a trip for a Copa Sudamericana match or a Copa Libertadores match, here is an honest comparison from the traveling fan's perspective. Both tournaments offer extraordinary experiences, but they cater to different priorities, budgets, and comfort levels.

Factor Copa Sudamericana Copa Libertadores

Ticket Availability Good to moderate Moderate to nearly impossible
Trip Cost (Budget) $80-400 USD $150-1,000 USD
Stadium Size 8,000-52,000 20,000-80,000
Atmosphere Intensity High (intimate, personal) Extreme (overwhelming, volcanic)
Safety Level Generally safe with precautions Requires serious preparation
First-Timer Friendly Excellent Challenging

The bottom line: if you want prestige, the highest-level football, and an atmosphere that borders on terrifying in its intensity, the Libertadores is the tournament. If you want affordability, accessibility, authentic discovery of unknown cities, and an atmosphere that is passionate without being overwhelming, the Sudamericana is your competition. Many experienced traveling fans alternate between the two, or use the Sudamericana as a warm-up for the Libertadores later in the season. For your first continental football trip, we unequivocally recommend starting with the Sudamericana.

This accessibility is one of the Sudamericana's biggest advantages for casual and first-time travelers.
Yes, with standard precautions. The Copa Sudamericana is generally safer for visiting fans than the Libertadores due to the lower stakes, smaller crowds, and less intense rivalries. That said, basic safety principles always apply: travel in groups, keep team colors in your bag until you are at the stadium, use ride-hailing apps instead of street taxis, carry only the cash you need, follow police instructions on matchday, and have a clear exit plan. For knockout rounds, police may designate specific routes for visiting fans -- always use them. Avoid lingering outside the stadium after the match. The Copa Sudamericana was created in 2002, with San Lorenzo of Argentina winning the inaugural edition. The tournament has been won by clubs from Argentina, Brazil, Ecuador, and Peru, among others. Notable champions include Cienciano of Peru (2003, defeating River Plate in a legendary final), Athletico Paranaense (2018, 2021), Independiente del Valle (2019, 2022), and Defensa y Justicia (2020). The 2016 edition was awarded to Chapecoense following the tragic plane crash that killed most of the squad en route to the final. Argentine clubs have won the most titles overall, but Ecuadorian clubs have dominated the modern era of the competition.
