Borders Logistics

How to Cross Borders for a Match Without Problems

Everything you need to know about navigating Latin American land borders, ferry crossings, and international checkpoints when you are traveling to support your team in another country. Documents, routes, costs, and the kind of street-level intel that keeps your away-day on track.

The Reality of Borders in South America

If you have only ever traveled within your own country to watch football, crossing an international border for the first time can seem intimidating. The truth is that border crossings in South America are, in most cases, far more straightforward than people expect, provided you understand the regional agreements that make the continent one of the most permeable regions in the world for travelers. The key is knowing which framework applies to your situation and planning accordingly.

The single most important concept for any traveling fan to understand is Mercosur. The Southern Common Market, established in 1991, includes Argentina, Brazil, Uruguay, Paraguay, and Bolivia as full members. Chile, Colombia, Ecuador, and Peru hold associate member status. For citizens of these countries, travel within the bloc is remarkably simple: in most cases, a valid national identity document (your DNI, RG, or cedula) is all you need. No passport, no visa, no pre-registration. You show up at the border, present your ID, get your entry stamp, and you are on your way to the stadium.

Mercosur Advantage

Citizens of Mercosur full and associate member states can travel within the bloc using only their national ID card. This covers Argentina, Brazil, Uruguay, Paraguay, Bolivia, Chile, Colombia, Ecuador, and Peru. A passport is not required, though carrying one is always a good backup.

The Andean Community (CAN) provides a similar framework for Bolivia, Colombia, Ecuador, and Peru. Citizens of these four countries can travel freely between them using their national ID. When CAN and Mercosur overlap, as with Bolivia, Colombia, Ecuador, and Peru, you get the widest freedom of movement on the continent. A Colombian fan heading to Quito for a Copa Libertadores match needs nothing more than their cedula. A Peruvian fan going to La Paz for a Bolivian league match can cross with just their DNI.

The picture changes when you move outside these blocs. Venezuela, while technically a Mercosur member, has complex entry requirements that fluctuate with its political situation, so always check the latest rules before planning a trip. Central American countries like Costa Rica, Panama, Honduras, Guatemala, and El Salvador have their own agreements through the CA-4 pact (Guatemala, El Salvador, Honduras, and Nicaragua), but South American citizens generally need a passport to enter these countries. And then there is the big one: the Mexico-United States border, which is a completely different universe in terms of documentation, wait times, and bureaucratic scrutiny. We cover that route in detail further down this guide.

Understanding the political geography is only half the equation. The other half is understanding how borders actually work on the ground. In South America, most land borders operate with two separate checkpoints: an emigration post on the side you are leaving, and an immigration post on the side you are entering. Some borders have these posts right next to each other; others have them separated by several kilometers, sometimes even requiring a short bus ride or taxi between the two. At some borders, like the famous Puente de la Amistad between Ciudad del Este (Paraguay) and Foz do Iguacu (Brazil), the pedestrian and vehicular flows create a chaotic environment that can be overwhelming on your first visit. At others, like the quiet Tacna-Arica crossing between Peru and Chile, the process is orderly and almost entirely painless.

The bottom line: South America is built for overland travel, and the frameworks in place make it relatively easy for citizens of the continent to cross borders. But "easy" does not mean "unprepared." The rest of this guide will give you everything you need to know so that your border crossing is the beginning of a great away-day, not the reason it falls apart.

Essential Documents

The first question every fan asks before an international away-day is: "What documents do I need?" The answer depends on three variables: your nationality, your destination, and the bilateral or multilateral agreements between those two countries. Below is a comprehensive overview, followed by a quick-reference matrix covering the most common fan travel corridors in the region.

Passport vs. National ID

For travel within the Mercosur and CAN zones, a valid national identity document is sufficient. In Argentina, this is the DNI (Documento Nacional de Identidad). In Brazil, the RG (Registro Geral) or the newer CNH with photo. In Colombia, the cedula de ciudadania. In Chile, the cedula de identidad. The critical word here is "valid": expired documents will be rejected at the border, no exceptions. We have seen fans turned away at the Argentina-Chile border because their DNI was expired by two weeks. Do not let this happen to you. Check your document's expiration date at least a month before your trip.

A passport is required for any travel outside the Mercosur/CAN zone, for citizens of countries that are not members of these blocs, and as a general best practice even when a national ID is technically sufficient. A passport is more universally recognized, carries more security features, and provides a convenient place for entry and exit stamps that can serve as proof of your travel history. If you travel internationally for football regularly, having a passport is non-negotiable.

Document Validity

Your national ID or passport must be valid (not expired) on the date of travel. Many countries also require that your passport has at least six months of remaining validity. Do not gamble on a document that is close to expiring. Renew it well in advance of your trip.

Pro Tip: Always Carry Both

Even when your national ID is sufficient, carry your passport as a backup. If your ID is damaged, cracked, or questioned by a border officer, a passport can save your trip. Keep photocopies (physical and digital) of both documents stored separately from the originals.

Other Documents Worth Having

Beyond your primary identification, a few supplementary documents can make your crossing smoother. Carry a printed or digital copy of your match ticket or reservation confirmation. While border officials rarely ask for this, it can help establish the purpose of your visit if you are questioned. Some fans also carry a printed hotel reservation and a return transport ticket to demonstrate they are genuine short-stay visitors, not attempting to immigrate. If you are driving across a border, you will need your vehicle registration, driver's license, and typically an international vehicle permit or temporary import authorization. For Mercosur countries, a national driver's license from any member state is generally accepted, but an international driving permit is advisable if you are traveling beyond the bloc.

Most Common Border Routes for Fans

Not all border crossings are created equal. Some are fast, modern, and painless; others are chaotic, slow, and test your patience. The routes below are the ones that football fans use most frequently, and each comes with its own quirks, tips, and logistics that we have documented firsthand.

Argentina ↔ Uruguay via Buquebus

This is the most popular and arguably the most enjoyable international crossing for football fans in South America. The Buquebus ferry connects Buenos Aires directly to Colonia del Sacramento (1 hour) or Montevideo (2.5 hours by direct ferry, or 3.5 hours by combined bus-ferry service). For Argentine fans heading to a Penarol or Nacional match, or Uruguayan fans crossing for a Boca or River match, this route is a ritual.

Transport: Buquebus operates high-speed catamarans and conventional ferries from the Buquebus Terminal in Puerto Madero, Buenos Aires. There is also Colonia Express, a budget alternative that runs smaller ferries to Colonia del Sacramento. From Colonia, regular buses connect to Montevideo in about 2.5 hours.

Cost: Buquebus direct to Montevideo runs from 50 to 120 USD round-trip depending on the class and how far in advance you book. Colonia Express is cheaper at around 30 to 60 USD round-trip to Colonia, plus another 10 to 15 USD for the bus to Montevideo. Budget option: the slow ferry (around 35 USD round-trip) plus bus.

Time: Buenos Aires to Montevideo is 2.5 to 4 hours total depending on the service. Immigration is processed at the terminal before boarding and again on arrival, so arrive at least 90 minutes before departure.

Buquebus Tip

Book online at least two weeks ahead for Copa Libertadores matchdays. Ferries sell out completely when Buenos Aires or Montevideo clubs play each other internationally. If you miss the direct ferry, the Colonia Express to Colonia plus a bus is a reliable Plan B.

Argentina ↔ Chile via Mendoza-Santiago

The Mendoza-Santiago corridor runs through the famous Cristo Redentor pass in the Andes, one of the most dramatic border crossings on the continent. Argentine and Chilean fans use this route constantly for matches between clubs like Boca Juniors, River Plate, Colo-Colo, and Universidad de Chile, especially during Copa Libertadores group stages.

Transport: International buses operated by companies like Andesmar, CATA Internacional, Pullman Bus, and Turbus depart daily from Mendoza's main terminal and arrive at Santiago's Terminal Alameda. The journey crosses the Andes at an altitude of over 3,000 meters, passing through the Los Libertadores tunnel. Direct flights from Buenos Aires to Santiago (2 hours) bypass the border entirely, but cost significantly more.

Cost: Bus tickets run 25 to 60 USD one-way. Budget flights from Buenos Aires to Santiago can be found for 80 to 200 USD round-trip if booked in advance.

Time: The bus takes 6 to 8 hours under normal conditions. In winter (June to September), the pass can close due to snow, sometimes for days. Always check road conditions before traveling in winter. Immigration is handled at a combined checkpoint (the Complejo Los Libertadores on the Chilean side and the Complejo Horcones on the Argentine side) partway through the mountain crossing.

Winter Warning

The Cristo Redentor pass closes regularly due to snow and wind between June and September. If your match falls during these months, always have a backup plan. A budget flight from Buenos Aires to Santiago is the safest alternative. Do not count on the pass being open.

Argentina ↔ Brasil

The Argentina-Brazil border is one of the busiest on the continent, and there are multiple crossing points depending on your destination. The most common for football fans are: Puerto Iguazu (Argentina) to Foz do Iguacu (Brazil) via the Puente Tancredo Neves, which connects to southern Brazil and is a gateway to matches in Porto Alegre (Gremio, Internacional) and Curitiba (Athletico Paranaense); and Paso de los Libres (Argentina) to Uruguaiana (Brazil), a crossing used by fans heading to Porto Alegre via overland routes.

Transport: International buses run daily from Buenos Aires to Porto Alegre (18 to 20 hours), Curitiba (22 hours), and Sao Paulo (26 to 30 hours). Companies include Pluma, Crucero del Norte, and JBL Turismo. Budget flights from Buenos Aires to Porto Alegre or Sao Paulo are often the smarter choice for distances beyond Foz do Iguacu.

Cost: Buses range from 40 to 100 USD one-way depending on distance and service class. Budget flights start at around 80 to 150 USD one-way on carriers like Gol, LATAM, and Aerolineas Argentinas.

Time: The Puerto Iguazu to Foz crossing takes about 30 minutes if there is no queue. Buses to Porto Alegre from Buenos Aires take 18 to 20 hours. Flights from Buenos Aires to Sao Paulo take about 2.5 hours.

Colombia ↔ Ecuador

The Ipiales-Tulcan crossing on the Pan-American Highway is the main land border between Colombia and Ecuador, and it is the route that fans of clubs like Deportivo Cali, Atletico Nacional, LDU Quito, and Barcelona SC use for international matches. The crossing itself is relatively straightforward, with both immigration posts located close together at the Rumichaca bridge.

Transport: International buses from Bogota or Cali to Quito operate daily. From Cali, the journey to Quito takes about 12 to 14 hours. Companies include Expreso Bolivariano and Flota Imbabura. Shared taxis (colectivos) from Ipiales to Tulcan are cheap and fast once you are at the border.

Cost: Bus from Cali to Quito runs 30 to 50 USD one-way. Shared taxi across the border costs about 2 USD per person.

Time: The border crossing itself takes 30 minutes to 1 hour. Total Cali to Quito journey is 12 to 14 hours by bus.

Peru ↔ Chile: Tacna-Arica

The Tacna-Arica crossing is one of the smoothest land borders on the continent. The two cities are only 56 kilometers apart, and the crossing is used heavily by fans traveling between Peru and Chile for matches involving teams from Santiago, Lima, and the northern Chilean cities. Peruvian fans heading south for Copa Libertadores matches and Chilean fans heading to Lima both pass through here.

Transport: Shared taxis (colectivos) depart from both Tacna and Arica throughout the day and handle the entire crossing, including waiting for you at the border posts. It is the fastest and most convenient option. International buses from Lima to Santiago also pass through here but take significantly longer.

Cost: Colectivo from Tacna to Arica costs about 8 to 12 USD per person. The full Lima to Santiago bus journey runs 60 to 100 USD one-way.

Time: Tacna to Arica by colectivo takes 1.5 to 2 hours including border processing. Lima to Santiago by bus is about 30 to 36 hours.

Mexico ↔ USA

This is the most complex border crossing covered in this guide, and it requires the most preparation. Mexican fans traveling to MLS matches, US Open Cup games, or CONCACAF Champions Cup fixtures in US cities like Dallas, Los Angeles, Houston, and San Antonio need either a valid B1/B2 tourist visa or a Border Crossing Card (BCC, also known as a laser visa). The BCC allows entry up to 25 miles into the US border zone for up to 72 hours.

Transport: The main pedestrian crossings are at Tijuana-San Ysidro (for San Diego and Los Angeles), Ciudad Juarez-El Paso, Nuevo Laredo-Laredo, and Reynosa-McAllen. Cross-border bus services like Greyhound, FlixBus, and Turimex connect major Mexican border cities to US destinations. Many fans choose to walk across and take rideshare or public transit on the US side.

Cost: The pedestrian crossing itself is free (no toll), but you need a valid visa (B1/B2 visa costs around 185 USD in application fees). Transport from Tijuana to downtown San Diego costs 5 to 10 USD by trolley. Cross-border buses to Los Angeles run 20 to 40 USD one-way.

Time: Pedestrian wait times at San Ysidro can range from 30 minutes to 3 hours depending on the day and time. Weekend mornings and Friday evenings are the worst. CBX (Cross Border Xpress), a skybridge connecting Tijuana Airport to the US side, is an alternative if you are flying into Tijuana.

US Entry Warning

US Customs and Border Protection (CBP) officers have broad discretion to deny entry. Having a valid visa does not guarantee admission. Be prepared to explain the purpose of your visit (attending a football match) and show evidence such as your match ticket, hotel booking, and return transport. Stay calm, answer questions directly, and do not joke about your intentions.

Border Transportation

Getting to the border is one thing. Getting across it efficiently and continuing to your destination is another. The transport options available to you depend on the specific route, but across Latin America, you will generally encounter four main modes: international buses, ferries, budget flights, and shared taxis (colectivos). Each has its strengths and weaknesses, and savvy traveling fans know when to use which.

International buses are the backbone of cross-border fan travel in South America. Companies like Andesmar, Pluma, Crucero del Norte, CATA Internacional, Pullman Bus, and Turbus operate routes that cross borders seamlessly, handling the immigration process as part of the journey. The bus stops at the border, everyone gets off to process their documents, and then the bus continues. Overnight (cama or semi-cama) services are the most popular choice because they save you a night's accommodation. The downside: journey times are long, and delays at the border can push your arrival time back significantly.

Ferries are specific to a few routes, most notably Buenos Aires to Montevideo/Colonia. They offer a comfortable, scenic crossing and handle immigration at the terminals, which feels more like an airport experience than a land border.

Budget flights have become increasingly competitive in Latin America thanks to carriers like JetSMART, SKY Airline, Viva Aerobus, Flybondi, and Gol. For distances over 1,000 km, a budget flight is often only marginally more expensive than a bus ticket and saves you 12 to 24 hours of travel time. Immigration is handled at the airport, which is generally faster and more organized than a land border.

Shared taxis (colectivos) are the go-to option for short border crossings where the two cities are close together, like Tacna-Arica, Ipiales-Tulcan, or Ciudad del Este-Foz do Iguacu. They are cheap, fast, and run frequently. The driver usually waits while you process your documents, which means you do not have to worry about catching another vehicle on the other side.

Money-Saving Tip

For distances over 1,000 km, always compare the bus price with budget flights. JetSMART, Flybondi, and SKY often run promotions that make flying cheaper than a semi-cama bus ticket once you factor in the hotel night you save by not spending 20 hours on the road. Set fare alerts on Google Flights or Skyscanner at least a month before your match.

Practical Tips at the Border

Knowing the route and having the right documents is the foundation. But there are dozens of small details that can make the difference between a smooth crossing and an unnecessary headache. Here is everything we have learned from years of crossing borders for football.

Cash in Both Currencies

Always carry cash in the currency of the country you are entering, even if it is just a small amount. You will need it for transport from the border to your destination, for food, for a SIM card, and for any unexpected expense. ATMs at border posts are unreliable: some do not exist, others are frequently out of cash or out of service. The best approach is to exchange a modest amount before you cross. Currency exchange houses at the border itself tend to offer terrible rates, so if possible, exchange in a city beforehand. In Argentina, the "blue dollar" parallel exchange rate means you get significantly more pesos by exchanging USD cash in certain places than by using your bank card. Research the specific currency situation for your destination before traveling.

SIM Cards and Connectivity

Your home country's mobile plan will probably not work in the destination country, or if it does, roaming charges will be brutal. Buy a local prepaid SIM card as soon as you cross the border. In most South American countries, you can get a SIM with 10 to 15 GB of data for 5 to 10 USD from vendors near the border or at the first town you reach. Having data connectivity is essential for navigation, rideshare apps, messaging your group, and checking real-time match information. Alternatively, download offline maps of your destination city in Google Maps or Maps.me before you leave home.

Customs and Prohibited Items

Customs regulations vary by country, but some rules are universal. Do not bring fresh fruit, meat, or dairy products across any South American border, especially when entering Chile, which has the strictest agricultural controls on the continent (the SAG inspectors at Chilean borders are thorough and the fines are real). Do not carry any kind of pyrotechnics, flares, or smoke bombs. These are confiscated and can get you detained. Alcohol limits vary but are generally 1 to 2 liters of spirits per person. Cigarettes are usually limited to 200 (one carton). Large sums of cash (typically over 10,000 USD or equivalent) must be declared.

Chile Agricultural Controls

Chile's SAG (Servicio Agricola y Ganadero) conducts mandatory agricultural inspections at every border crossing. Do not bring any fresh food, fruit, vegetables, meat, seeds, or dairy. Fines start at 200 USD and can go much higher. This rule is enforced aggressively. If the bus you are on serves food with fresh items, finish or dispose of them before reaching the border.

Timing Your Crossing

If you have flexibility, cross the border the day before the match. This eliminates the stress of potential delays, gives you time to settle in, explore the host city, and join the pre-match atmosphere properly. If you must cross on matchday, go as early in the morning as possible. Border wait times increase throughout the day and spike in the afternoon when other fans are making the same crossing. For major international matches (Copa Libertadores knockouts, national team qualifiers), expect the border to be significantly busier than usual.

Football Merchandise and Fan Items

Wearing your team's jersey at the border is generally fine, but use common sense. If you are crossing a border known for rivalry tensions, consider wearing a neutral shirt and changing after you arrive. As for bringing fan items across, small flags, scarves, and banners without poles pass through without issue. Large flags with poles, drums, megaphones, and especially any kind of pyrotechnic device will be confiscated. If you are traveling with a large organized group, your supporter association will typically coordinate with the clubs on both sides to pre-authorize the entry of drums and large banners through official channels.

Common Problems and How to Solve Them

Even with the best preparation, problems can arise at the border. The key is knowing how to handle them calmly and efficiently. Here are the most common issues that football fans encounter and how to resolve them.

Document Issues

The number one problem: expired documents. If your ID or passport is expired, you will not cross. Period. There is no grace period, no exception, and no amount of explaining that you have a Copa Libertadores semifinal ticket will change the officer's mind. The second most common issue is a damaged document: a cracked or delaminated national ID, a passport with water damage, or a photo that no longer resembles you. Border officers have the right to reject any document they consider unreliable. The solution: check your documents well in advance and renew anything that is close to expiring or in poor condition.

If you are denied due to a document issue, your only option is to return home and get a replacement. Some countries offer emergency or same-day document renewal services, but these are available only at specific offices and are not guaranteed. This is why we always recommend carrying both your national ID and your passport: if one is rejected, the other may save your trip.

Denied Entry

Beyond document issues, entry can be denied for several reasons: being on a football violence registry (Argentina's "derecho de admision" database is shared with neighboring countries during major tournaments), having outstanding legal issues in the destination country, being unable to demonstrate sufficient funds for your stay, or simply failing to convince the officer that your visit is genuinely temporary. If you are denied entry, stay calm. You have the right to know the specific reason. Ask for it in writing if possible. Contact your country's nearest consulate for assistance. Do not argue aggressively with border officials; it will only make things worse.

Football Violence Registries

Argentina, Brazil, and Chile maintain databases of individuals banned from attending football matches. During high-profile international fixtures, these databases are shared between countries, and individuals on the list can be denied entry at the border. If you have been flagged in one of these systems, resolve your status before attempting to travel internationally for football.

Excessive Wait Times

Wait times are unpredictable. On a normal weekday, most South American land borders process you in under an hour. On a Copa Libertadores matchday, when buses full of fans arrive simultaneously, you can be looking at 2 to 4 hours. The worst scenario is when one side's immigration system goes down (it happens more often than you would think), which can create multi-hour backups. Strategies for managing this: cross the day before, cross early in the morning, bring snacks and water, fully charge your phone, download entertainment for the wait, and stay patient. Losing your temper at a border never helps.

Currency Exchange Pitfalls

The currency exchange houses at land borders are notorious for terrible rates. They know you are a captive audience. The solution: exchange only a small amount at the border (enough for immediate transport and food) and do the rest at a proper exchange house or ATM in the destination city. In Argentina, the situation is more complex because of the difference between the official exchange rate and the parallel "blue dollar" rate. If you are entering Argentina with USD cash, you can get significantly more pesos by exchanging at a "cueva" (informal exchange house) in the city, but these operate in a legal gray area. Research the current situation before your trip. In Brazil, look for "casas de cambio" in the city center rather than at the border. In Chile, exchange houses on Calle Agustinas in Santiago offer competitive rates.
