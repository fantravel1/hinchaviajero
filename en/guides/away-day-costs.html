How Much Does an Away-Day Really Cost in Latin America?

Real numbers, real routes, real budgets. The definitive cost breakdown for every traveling fan -- from the backpacker counting every dollar to the supporter who wants a little more comfort. No guessing, just data.

The Question Every Fan Asks

Before any away-day, before the excitement of planning the route or imagining what the stadium will feel like, there is one question that stops most fans in their tracks: how much is this going to cost me? It is the most practical question in football travel, and yet it is the one with the least reliable answers available online. Search for "away-day budget" and you will find vague ranges, outdated figures from European contexts, and almost nothing specific to Latin America. That changes here.

There is a persistent myth in the football travel world that following your team on the road requires deep pockets. That away-days are a luxury reserved for fans who can casually drop hundreds of dollars on a weekend trip. This is flatly wrong -- at least in Latin America. The continent offers some of the most affordable football travel on the planet, but only if you know where the money actually goes and how to manage it intelligently. A fan who plans well can follow their team to another city for the price of a decent dinner in London or Madrid. A fan who plans poorly can spend five times that amount and still have a miserable experience.

The truth is that the cost of an away-day is not a single number. It is a combination of five distinct categories, each with its own variables, its own tricks for saving money, and its own traps for the uninformed. Understanding these categories -- and knowing the real prices in each country -- is the difference between a sustainable habit of away-day travel and a one-time adventure that blows your budget.

"I thought away-days were something only rich fans could afford. Then I took my first overnight bus to Rosario, spent 40 dollars total, and realized I'd been overthinking it for years. The hardest part isn't the money -- it's deciding to go."

This guide is built on real data. We have collected prices from actual trips taken by fans across six countries in 2025 and 2026. Every number you see here reflects what traveling fans actually paid -- not what a travel agency quotes, not what a tourism board advertises, and not what someone vaguely remembers from a trip three years ago. We update these figures regularly because Latin American economies are volatile, and a price from six months ago can be wildly off today.

Whether you are planning your first domestic away-day on a shoestring budget or calculating the total cost of a Copa Libertadores trip across borders, this guide will give you the numbers you need. Let us break it all down.

Cost Breakdown: The 5 Categories

Every away-day budget, regardless of destination or travel style, breaks down into five fundamental categories. Understanding the weight of each category is the first step to controlling your spending. Here is the framework that applies across every country and every route.

Transport (40-50% of your budget)

Transport is the single largest expense on any away-day, and it is also the category where smart planning yields the biggest savings. In Latin America, you have three main options: overnight buses, low-cost flights, and shared ground transport (car pools or organized fan caravans). Each one has a sweet spot depending on distance and timing.

Overnight buses are the backbone of away-day travel in South America. A semi-cama (semi-reclining) ticket from Buenos Aires to Rosario costs 8-15 USD. A full cama (fully reclining sleeper) from Lima to Arequipa runs 15-30 USD. From Santiago to Valparaiso, you are looking at 5-10 USD. These buses run frequently, depart from central terminals, and the cama category gives you a seat that reclines nearly flat -- meaning you sleep on the road and save a night of accommodation. For distances under 10 hours, the overnight bus is almost always the most economical choice.

Low-cost flights have transformed the equation for longer routes. JetSMART, SKY Airline, Volaris, and VivaAerobus regularly offer domestic fares between 20 and 60 USD one way if booked 3-4 weeks ahead. The catch: baggage fees can double the price if you are not traveling light. A backpack as personal item is usually free. A checked bag can add 15-30 USD each way. The rule of thumb: if the bus takes more than 10 hours and a low-cost flight is available for under 50 USD, fly.

Shared ground transport -- whether an organized fan caravan by bus or splitting fuel costs in a car with 3-4 friends -- can bring your per-person transport cost down to 5-15 USD for routes under 6 hours. Many supporter groups (barras and peñas) organize collective trips for important matches and sell seats at cost. Follow your club's fan organizations on social media for announcements.

The Booking Window Sweet Spot

For both buses and low-cost flights, the optimal booking window is 2 to 4 weeks before departure. Earlier than that, prices are not yet at their lowest. Later than that, demand-based pricing kicks in and fares climb steeply -- especially for routes to cities hosting big matches. Midweek departures (Tuesday to Thursday) are consistently cheaper than Friday or Saturday travel.

Accommodation (20-30% of your budget)

Accommodation is the second-largest cost, but it is also the most elastic. You can spend zero (sleeping on the bus, crashing with a friend) or you can spend 80 USD a night on a hotel near the stadium. Most traveling fans land somewhere in between.

Hostels remain the default for solo travelers and small groups. Dorm beds in major Latin American cities run 8-18 USD per night depending on the city. Buenos Aires and Bogota sit at the lower end (8-12 USD). Santiago and Sao Paulo are higher (14-18 USD). Hostelworld, Booking.com, and Google Maps reviews are your tools for finding a clean, safe bed in a well-connected neighborhood.

Airbnb and short-term rentals become the better deal when you are traveling in a group of 3 or more. A two-bedroom apartment in a decent neighborhood can cost 30-60 USD per night, which split four ways comes to 8-15 USD per person -- often cheaper than a hostel dorm and with the added benefit of a kitchen (saving you money on meals) and more privacy. Book at least two weeks ahead; matchday weekends drive up demand in stadium neighborhoods.

The overnight bus trick: If your away-day is a day trip or a quick turnaround, the overnight bus eliminates accommodation costs entirely. You travel overnight, arrive in the morning, watch the match, and catch the return bus that same night. Your "hotel" is the bus seat. This is how the majority of budget-conscious fans in Argentina, Chile, and Peru travel for domestic away-days under 8 hours.

Match Tickets (10-20% of your budget)

The price of getting into the stadium varies enormously depending on the country, the match, and the section. General admission (standing terraces or "popular" sections) is consistently the cheapest option and is where the vast majority of traveling fans watch the game. Reserved seating costs more but is rarely worth the premium for the away experience -- the atmosphere in the standing section is what you came for.

General admission prices by country: Argentina: 5-15 USD. Colombia: 5-12 USD. Peru: 5-15 USD. Mexico: 10-25 USD. Chile: 10-20 USD. Brazil: 15-35 USD. These are regular league match prices. Cup matches, derbies, and international competitions (Copa Libertadores, Copa Sudamericana) can cost 2-5 times more, with visiting section tickets for Libertadores knockout rounds reaching 30-80 USD.

Always buy through official channels: the club's box office, the club's website, or authorized ticketing platforms (Ticketmaster in Mexico, Passline in Chile, TodoEntradas in Argentina). Resale markets and social media sellers carry serious scam risk. Read our Tickets Without Scams guide for a full breakdown of safe purchasing methods.

Membership Requirements

In Argentina, Brazil, and Uruguay, many league matches require club membership (socio status) to purchase tickets. Visitor section tickets are allocated through the visiting club and sell out fast. If you plan to attend away matches regularly, becoming a socio of your club is worth the small monthly fee (typically 3-10 USD) for priority ticket access.

Food & Drink (10-15% of your budget)

Food is where Latin American away-days truly shine in terms of value. The street food culture surrounding stadiums across the continent means you can eat well for very little -- if you know where to look. The rule is simple: eat where the locals eat, not where the tourists eat.

Street food near stadiums: A choripan (grilled sausage sandwich) outside an Argentine stadium costs 1-3 USD. A plate of anticuchos (grilled beef heart skewers) near a Peruvian stadium runs 2-4 USD. Tacos al pastor from a cart outside a Mexican venue: 1-2 USD for a generous serving. Arepas near a Colombian ground: 1-3 USD. These are not just budget options -- they are cultural experiences that are part of the matchday ritual.

Full meals: A "menu del dia" (set lunch) at a local market or working-class restaurant costs 2-5 USD in Peru, Colombia, Argentina, and Bolivia. In Chile and Brazil, expect 5-10 USD. A sit-down dinner at a mid-range restaurant runs 8-15 USD across most countries. If you have access to a kitchen (Airbnb), cooking your own meals can cut food costs to 5-8 USD per day.

Drinks: A beer at a bar near the stadium costs 1-3 USD in Argentina, Colombia, and Peru, and 3-5 USD in Chile and Brazil. Water and soft drinks from street vendors are typically under 1 USD. Inside the stadium, prices are inflated -- buy your drinks before you enter.

Extras (5-10% of your budget)

The extras category covers everything that does not fit neatly into the other four: local SIM cards or data plans (5-10 USD), souvenirs and scarves (5-20 USD), tips, public transit within the city (1-5 USD per day), a portable battery charger, and the ever-important emergency fund. This last item is non-negotiable. Always carry a buffer of at least 20% above your planned budget. Buses break down, flights get canceled, and you might need a last-minute hotel room or a taxi in the middle of the night. The emergency fund is not a luxury -- it is insurance.

Exchange Rate Advantage

If you are traveling from a country with a stronger currency (USD, EUR, GBP) or even from Chile or Brazil into Argentina, Colombia, or Peru, your money goes significantly further. Always check the current exchange rate before planning, and bring cash in USD as a backup -- it is widely accepted for informal exchange across the continent and often gets you a better rate than ATMs.

Case Study: How Much Does It Really Cost to Travel to Lima?

Numbers in tables are useful, but nothing illustrates real costs like a concrete example. Let us walk through a detailed, day-by-day breakdown of an actual away-day trip: traveling from Santiago, Chile, to Lima, Peru, to watch an international Copa Libertadores group stage match. This is a mid-range budget -- not the absolute cheapest, but not luxury either. Comfortable, safe, and realistic.

Day 0 (Wednesday Evening) -- Departure from Santiago

Flight Santiago to Lima: $85 USD (JetSMART, booked 3 weeks ahead, backpack only as personal item). Departure at 10:30 PM, arrival at 1:00 AM Lima time. Pre-flight dinner at the airport: $12 USD. Taxi from Lima airport to hostel in Miraflores via Uber: $8 USD.

Day 0 Total: $105 USD

Day 1 (Thursday) -- Matchday

Breakfast at hostel: Free (included). Lunch at a local market in Surquillo: $3 USD for a full menu del dia (soup, main course, drink, dessert). Local bus to Ate district (near Estadio Monumental): $0.50 USD. Pre-match street food (anticuchos and chicha morada): $4 USD. Match ticket (visiting section, purchased through the club 2 weeks ahead): $25 USD. Post-match Uber from stadium area back to Miraflores: $7 USD. Late-night celebratory cevicheria with a beer: $10 USD.

Hostel (dorm bed in Miraflores): $12 USD per night.

Day 1 Total: $61.50 USD

Day 2 (Friday) -- Return

Breakfast at hostel: Free. Morning walk along the Malecon, coffee at a local cafe: $2 USD. Souvenir scarf from a street vendor: $5 USD. Lunch at a picantera near Parque Kennedy: $4 USD. Uber to airport: $8 USD. Return flight Lima to Santiago: $90 USD (booked same time as outbound). Airport snack: $3 USD.

Day 2 Total: $112 USD

What this tells us: An international Copa Libertadores away-day from Santiago to Lima -- including flights, a hostel night, food, the match ticket, and local transport -- came in under $280 USD at a mid-range comfort level. On a strict budget (overnight bus instead of flights, no hostel, street food only), the same trip could have cost as little as $80-120 USD. At premium level (business-class flight, hotel, restaurant meals), it would have run $500-700 USD. The mid-range sweet spot gives you comfort, safety, and the full experience without breaking the bank.

10 Money-Saving Tricks Without Sacrificing the Experience

Saving money on an away-day does not mean having a worse time. In many cases, the budget-friendly choices are actually better experiences -- eating at a local market is more authentic than a tourist restaurant, and an overnight bus has its own romance that a one-hour flight cannot replicate. Here are ten proven strategies that seasoned traveling fans use to keep costs down without sacrificing any of the magic.

  1. Book transport 2-4 weeks ahead, never last minute. This single habit saves more money than any other. Last-minute bus and flight prices can be 2-3 times higher than advance fares. Set a calendar reminder two weeks before the match date and buy that day. For low-cost airlines, Tuesday and Wednesday tend to have the cheapest fares.
  2. Take overnight buses for routes under 12 hours. You save on transport cost compared to a flight, and you eliminate one night of accommodation. A $15 USD bus cama ticket replaces a $15 bus ride plus a $12 hostel bed. That is $12 saved on every trip, and you arrive in the morning with the whole day ahead of you. The bus is not just transport -- it is your hotel room on wheels.
  3. Travel in groups and split accommodations. An Airbnb that costs $40 per night split among four friends is $10 each -- cheaper than most hostel dorms and with the bonus of a kitchen, a living room, and privacy. Even splitting a taxi or Uber to the stadium among four people cuts that cost by 75%. Group travel is the single most effective multiplier of savings across every category.
  4. Eat at local markets, not restaurants near the stadium. Stadium-adjacent restaurants mark up prices by 50-100% on matchday. Walk ten blocks in any direction and the same meal is half the price. Better yet, find the municipal market -- a full lunch at a market stall in Lima, Bogota, or Buenos Aires costs $2-5 USD and is often the best meal you will eat all trip. Ask locals where they eat. Follow the crowd, not the signs.
  5. Carry cash in local currency and exchange smartly. ATM fees abroad add up fast -- $3-5 per withdrawal plus unfavorable exchange rates. Instead, bring USD or EUR cash and exchange at reputable cambios (exchange houses) in city centers. In Argentina, the parallel "blue dollar" rate can give you 20-40% more purchasing power than official bank rates. Avoid exchanging money at airports or border crossings, where rates are always the worst.
  6. Travel with only a backpack to avoid airline baggage fees. Low-cost airlines charge $15-30 each way for checked bags and sometimes even for cabin-sized carry-ons. A 40-liter backpack that fits as a personal item under the seat is all you need for a 2-3 day away trip. Pack one change of clothes, your jersey, toiletries, a phone charger, and your documents. You do not need more than that. The lighter you travel, the more money stays in your pocket.
  7. Join organized fan caravans for domestic matches. Supporter groups negotiate bulk rates for buses, and the per-person cost is often 30-50% less than buying an individual ticket. Follow your club's official fan accounts on social media -- they post caravan details 1-2 weeks before the match. Beyond the savings, traveling with the hinchada is part of the experience. The bus ride itself becomes a pre-match event.
  8. Use free hostel breakfasts and kitchen access relentlessly. Most hostels include a basic breakfast (bread, eggs, coffee, fruit). That is one meal sorted at zero cost. If your hostel or Airbnb has a kitchen, buy groceries at a local supermarket for dinner instead of eating out. A loaf of bread, some ham, cheese, and fruit for tomorrow's snacks costs $3-5 USD total and covers multiple meals.
  9. Buy match tickets through official club channels, never resellers. Resale platforms and informal sellers charge premiums of 50-200% above face value. Official club channels sell at face value, and many clubs offer discounted rates for socios (members). If you attend more than two away matches per year, the $3-10 monthly socio fee pays for itself in ticket savings alone. Plus, you avoid the very real risk of buying a fake ticket and losing everything.
  10. Plan your matchday city transit before you arrive. Research the local bus or metro routes from your accommodation to the stadium in advance. Public transit costs $0.25-1.00 per ride in most Latin American cities. A taxi or Uber for the same route might cost $5-10. Over the course of a two-day trip with multiple trips (to and from the stadium, to the market, to the airport), the difference between public transit and ride-hailing apps can be $15-25 USD. Download the local transit app and load your transit card with credit as soon as you arrive.
The Compound Effect

None of these tricks saves a life-changing amount on its own. But stack them together and the effect compounds dramatically. A fan who applies all ten strategies consistently can attend 3-4 away matches for the same total cost that a fan with no strategy spends on a single trip. Over the course of a season, that is the difference between being a regular traveling fan and being a once-a-year visitor.

When It's Worth Spending More

This guide has spent a lot of time talking about how to save money, and rightly so -- budget management is the foundation of sustainable away-day travel. But there are moments when spending more is not just justified, it is the right call. Knowing when to loosen the purse strings is just as important as knowing when to tighten them.

Finals and knockout rounds. If your team reaches a Copa Libertadores final, a league championship decider, or a derby with historic stakes, this is not the time to take the cheapest bus and skip the post-match celebration to save $10. These are once-in-a-lifetime experiences. Book a proper hotel so you can rest before and after. Buy the best ticket section you can afford. Take the flight instead of the 18-hour bus so you arrive fresh and ready. The emotional return on investment for a final is exponentially higher than a regular league match, and you want to experience it at your best, not exhausted and stressed from cutting every corner.

Safety-first situations. If you are traveling to a city you do not know well, to a rivalry match with a high-tension atmosphere, or arriving very late at night, this is the time to pay for the Uber instead of walking, the hotel instead of the hostel in a sketchy neighborhood, or the direct flight instead of the bus that arrives at 3 AM. Your safety is not an area where savings make sense. A $10 Uber at midnight is infinitely cheaper than the consequences of walking through an unfamiliar neighborhood after a heated derby.

Comfort for long distances. A 20-hour bus in semi-cama (semi-reclining) saves you $10-15 compared to the full cama (flat bed) option. But arriving with a wrecked back and zero sleep means you spend the match in survival mode instead of enjoying it. For any bus journey over 10 hours, the upgrade to cama class is worth every extra dollar. Similarly, for international trips that span multiple days, investing in a comfortable bed and a decent meal the night before the match is an investment in the quality of the entire experience.

When traveling with a partner or family. If your partner, a family member, or a friend who is not a hardcore fan is joining you, spending more on accommodation and comfort is the strategic move that ensures they have a good time -- and that they will be willing to come again. A miserable partner on a bargain-basement away-day means it will be your last away-day with them. A comfortable partner on a slightly pricier trip means you have a travel companion for years to come.

"I've done away-days on $30 and away-days on $300. The best experiences weren't always the most expensive ones, but the worst experiences were always the ones where I cut costs in the wrong places. Cheap transport is fine. Cheap food is great. Cheap safety decisions are never worth it."
