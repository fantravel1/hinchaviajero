How to Travel as an Away Fan and Do It Right

The definitive guide for anyone planning their first — or fiftieth — away-day across Latin America. Real logistics, real costs, real culture. Everything the internet doesn't tell you about traveling as a visiting fan on this continent.

What Is an Away-Day and Why Should You Experience One?

In its simplest definition, an away-day is the experience of traveling to another city — or another country — to watch your team play as the visiting side. But if you reduce it to that definition, you miss the point entirely. An away-day is a pilgrimage. It is the conscious decision to leave your comfort zone, board a bus or a plane, cross borders both physical and cultural, and plant yourself in hostile territory for ninety minutes of collective emotion. It is, without exaggeration, one of the most powerful experiences a football fan can have.

In Latin America, away-day culture carries a weight and a texture that is fundamentally different from what you might encounter in Europe. European away culture, for all its passion, operates within a framework of efficient rail networks, standardized ticketing systems, and policing protocols that have been refined over decades. The infrastructure is there. In Latin America, the infrastructure is the adventure. Getting to the match is half the story. The 14-hour overnight bus through the Andes, the border crossing where nothing works like it should, the scramble for tickets in a language or dialect you barely understand, the pre-match asado in a stranger's backyard — these are not obstacles to the experience. They are the experience.

The emotional and cultural value of an away-day is difficult to overstate. When you travel to support your team in a stadium full of people who want you to lose, something fundamental happens. The bonds you form with the fellow travelers around you become unbreakable. The chants feel different when you are outnumbered ten to one. The collective roar of three hundred visiting fans drowning out a stadium of forty thousand is a feeling that no home match can replicate. And when you return home — exhausted, sunburned, broke, and hoarse — you carry a story that connects you to a tradition stretching back generations.

There is also a cultural dimension that goes beyond football. When you travel as a visiting fan in Latin America, you encounter cities from a vantage point no ordinary tourist ever gets. You eat where the locals eat, not where the guidebook tells you to. You learn the geography of a city through its football culture — which barrio is whose, which plaza is the gathering point, which corner has the best choripan or empanada. You see the mural art, hear the cumbia or vallenato bleeding out of someone's speaker at the pre-match, and feel the social fabric of a place in a way that no museum visit or walking tour could ever deliver. Football is the skeleton key to Latin American culture, and the away-day is how you use it.

Planning: Before You Leave Home

A good away-day is an improvised adventure. A great away-day is an improvised adventure that was carefully planned. The difference between the two is the difference between a story you tell for years and a disaster you want to forget. Here is how to get the planning right without killing the spontaneity.

Choose Your Match Strategically

Not every away match is created equal. Your first decision is which match to chase, and this is where strategy matters. Consider these factors before you commit:

  • Rivalry intensity: Derbies and historic rivalries produce the best atmospheres, but they also carry the highest risk for visiting fans. If this is your first away-day, consider starting with a lower-stakes match to learn the ropes before jumping into a Superclasico.
  • Weather and season: South American football calendars run year-round. A match in Buenos Aires in July means near-freezing temperatures in the stands. A match in Barranquilla in March means 35-degree heat and crushing humidity. Plan your gear accordingly.
  • Cost and distance: A domestic away-day by bus is dramatically cheaper than an international flight. Start local. Build experience. Then go big.
  • Índice Hincha™ score: Our editorial rating system scores the overall experience of attending a match as a visiting fan. Check the Índice Hincha for your target match to understand what you are signing up for — a score of 85+ means you are in for something unforgettable.
Pro Tip

Monitor the fixture schedule closely. In Latin American leagues, match dates and kickoff times are often confirmed only one to two weeks before the match. CONMEBOL and local federations can shift schedules for television, weather, or security reasons. Do not book non-refundable transport until the fixture is officially locked in.

Transportation — The Latin American Reality

Forget what you know about European rail passes and budget airline networks. Transportation for away-days in Latin America operates by its own rules. Here is what actually works:

Overnight buses are the backbone of away-day travel on this continent. In Argentina, companies like Flecha Bus, Chevallier, and Andesmar run semi-cama (semi-reclining) and cama (fully reclining) services between major cities. In Peru, Cruz del Sur and Oltursa are the gold standard. In Colombia, Bolivariano and Expreso Brasilia cover the major routes. In Brazil, Viacao 1001 and Catarinense operate the southeastern corridor. The key is understanding the tier system: cama ejecutivo is worth the extra money for trips over eight hours. You will arrive rested and ready for la previa. Semi-cama on a 14-hour haul means arriving wrecked.

Budget airlines have transformed the landscape in the last decade. In Colombia, Viva Air and Wingo offer fares under 50 USD one-way if you book early. In Argentina, Flybondi connects Buenos Aires with the interior at prices that compete with buses. In Mexico, Volaris and VivaAerobus are the budget kings. In Brazil, GOL and Azul run constant promotions. The catch: baggage fees can double your fare, schedule changes are common, and airports are rarely near stadiums. Factor in ground transport from the airport to the city center.

Fan caravans are a uniquely Latin American phenomenon. Many barras bravas and supporter groups organize bus caravans for big away matches. These are often the cheapest option and by far the most atmospheric — imagine fifty fans on a charter bus, flags hanging from every window, cumbia on the speakers, and a cooler full of beer in the aisle. The downside: you are on their schedule, not yours. Departure times are non-negotiable, stops are communal, and the return trip depends on the group's mood. If you can handle the chaos, a caravan is an unforgettable experience. Ask in the club's social media groups or fan forums for caravan details.

Smart Accommodation

Where you stay matters more than you think. The wrong neighborhood can mean a sketchy walk to the stadium, a surge-priced cab at midnight, or an area that becomes a no-go zone on matchday. The right neighborhood means you can walk to the ground, soak up the pre-match atmosphere on the street, and walk back safely after the final whistle.

Research the stadium's surroundings before you book anything. In many Latin American cities, the stadium sits in a working-class neighborhood that may feel unfamiliar to outsiders. That doesn't mean it is dangerous — it means you need to know which streets are fine and which to avoid, especially after dark. Our city-specific guides map safe corridors between accommodation zones and stadiums for every major ground on the continent.

Hostels vs. Airbnb: For solo travelers or small groups, hostels near the stadium district offer the cheapest beds and the best chance of meeting other traveling fans. In Buenos Aires, the San Telmo and La Boca hostels are legendary for pre-match gatherings. In Bogota, the La Candelaria hostels put you within metro reach of El Campin. Airbnb is better for groups of three or more — splitting a two-bedroom apartment in a safe neighborhood near the ground can be cheaper per person than a hostel and gives you a home base for la previa.

Proximity is king. If you can stay within walking distance of the stadium (under 2 km), do it. It eliminates the transport headache on matchday, lets you join the street atmosphere as it builds, and means you can walk back at your own pace after the match when ride-hailing apps are surging and public transport is overwhelmed.

Money Tip

Always carry cash in the local currency. Card acceptance near stadiums and in surrounding neighborhoods is unreliable across the continent. ATMs near grounds are often out of service on matchday. Withdraw cash in the city center before heading to the stadium zone.

Tickets — The Trickiest Part

Getting tickets for an away match in Latin America can be straightforward or it can be a labyrinth. It depends entirely on the country, the club, the rivalry, and whether the match has been classified as "high risk" by local authorities. Here is how to navigate it.

Official channels first, always. Every major club in Latin America sells tickets through at least one official channel: the club's box office (boleteria), the club's website, or an authorized third-party platform like Ticketmaster (Mexico), Passline (Chile), or Ticketek (Argentina). These are your safest options. Prices are set, the tickets are guaranteed, and you avoid the risk of getting turned away at the gate with a counterfeit or duplicated code.

Membership systems: In Argentina, the socio system means that many clubs only sell tickets to registered members. If you are an international visitor, this can be a hurdle. Some clubs offer temporary memberships or tourist packages for visiting fans — Boca Juniors and River Plate both have these. Research whether your target club offers a pathway for non-members before you travel.

Digital vs. physical tickets: The trend across the continent is moving toward digital tickets and QR codes, but not all stadiums are there yet. In Colombia and Chile, e-tickets are widely accepted. In Argentina, some clubs still use physical tickets that must be picked up in person. In Brazil, the Ingresso Rapido system is common. Always confirm the ticket format before matchday and make sure your phone is charged if you are using a digital ticket.

The away section: For classified matches (high-rivalry games), tickets for the visiting section are often sold exclusively through the visiting club, not the home club. This means you may need to buy your ticket in your home city before you travel. In some cases, the visiting allocation is deliberately limited or eliminated entirely by local police for safety reasons. Check the status of the visiting allocation before you plan your trip.

Scam Warning

Never buy tickets from social media sellers, WhatsApp groups from unknown contacts, or street scalpers outside the stadium. The most common scams include: duplicated QR codes that only work for the first person who scans them, counterfeit physical tickets, and sellers who take payment and disappear. If a deal looks too good to be true, it is. If someone approaches you outside the stadium offering tickets at face value or below, walk away. Counterfeit tickets are a continent-wide problem and the police will not help you get your money back.

Safety — Smart, Not Paranoid

Let's be direct: Latin American football has a reputation for being dangerous, and parts of that reputation are earned. But the reality is far more nuanced than the headlines suggest. Thousands of visiting fans travel to away matches across the continent every single week and come home with nothing worse than a sore throat. The difference between a fan who has a great time and a fan who has a bad one almost always comes down to preparation and awareness.

General principles: Travel in groups. Do not flash expensive electronics or jewelry near the stadium. Keep your phone in a zipped pocket. Carry only the cash you need for the day and leave the rest at your accommodation. Know the emergency number for the country you are in (911 works in most of Latin America, but not all). Have your accommodation's address written down in case your phone dies.

Jersey wearing advice: This is the question every first-time away-day traveler asks, and the answer depends entirely on the match. For low-stakes league matches with no major rivalry, wearing your team's jersey to and from the ground is usually fine. For derbies and high-rivalry matches, the calculus changes. In Argentina, wearing a Boca jersey through a River neighborhood (or vice versa) on matchday is asking for trouble. The general rule: wear your jersey inside the stadium, in the visiting section, surrounded by your people. Outside the ground, keep it in your bag. This is not cowardice — it is street intelligence.

Stadium entry and exit: Arrive at the stadium early — at least 90 minutes before kickoff for big matches. Entry queues can be chaotic, and security checks are thorough but slow. Do not bring prohibited items (check the stadium's list online beforehand). After the match, the visiting section is often held inside the stadium for 15 to 45 minutes to allow the home fans to disperse. Do not fight this — it is for your safety. Use the wait to celebrate or commiserate with your fellow travelers.

Post-match: When you are released from the stadium, follow the police corridor to the designated exit route for visiting fans. Do not wander off on your own into unfamiliar streets in the dark. If you have pre-booked a ride or have your car parked in a known location, head straight there. If you are using public transport, know which route to take before the match — cell signal around stadiums is often overwhelmed after a match ends.

Safety Checklist

• Travel in a group of at least two or three people
• Keep your jersey in your bag outside the stadium for high-rivalry matches
• Arrive early and leave through the designated visiting-fan exit
• Carry only what you need: cash, phone, ID, ticket
• Know the emergency number and your accommodation address by heart
• Pre-download offline maps of the stadium area
• Avoid engaging with home fans who are looking for confrontation
• Do not post your exact location on social media in real-time during high-risk matches

La Previa — The Sacred Pre-Match Ritual

If someone tells you that football starts at kickoff, they have never been to Latin America. On this continent, the match is merely the climax of a ritual that begins hours earlier. That ritual is called la previa, and it is sacred.

La previa is the collective gathering of fans before a match. It takes place in bars, on street corners, in plazas, in parking lots, and sometimes in fans' homes near the stadium. There is food — choripan in Argentina, bandeja paisa or empanadas in Colombia, anticuchos in Peru, tacos in Mexico. There is drink — Fernet with Coca-Cola in Argentina, aguardiente in Colombia, pisco sour in Peru, mezcal in Mexico, cerveza everywhere. There are drums, chants, smoke bombs, and a building energy that transforms a collection of strangers into a tribe.

For the visiting fan, la previa is where the away-day experience truly begins. Finding the right spot is key. In most cities, the visiting fans congregate in a known area — a specific bar, a plaza, or a stretch of street a few blocks from the stadium. These spots are often communicated through fan group social media accounts or WhatsApp chains. When you arrive, introduce yourself. Say where you traveled from. In Latin American football culture, the fact that you made the trip earns you immediate respect. You will be offered food, drink, and a place in the circle.

How to find the best previa spots: Follow the fan groups of your club on Twitter/X, Instagram, and Facebook. Many barras and supporter groups post the gathering point a day or two before the match. Local football podcasts and YouTube channels also share previa recommendations. If you are in a foreign country, ask at your hostel — the staff usually know exactly where the fans gather. When in doubt, follow the drums. Literally. If you hear drums and chanting, walk toward the sound.

Previa etiquette: Be generous. If someone shares their food or drink with you, reciprocate when you can. Do not get excessively drunk — you still need to navigate stadium entry, enjoy the match, and get home safely. Respect the space. If a group of hard-core ultras has claimed a corner, observe from a respectful distance unless invited in. Take photos, but ask first. And above all, sing. Even if you do not know all the words. The effort is what counts.

Match Day

You have planned, traveled, found your accommodation, and scouted the city. Now it is matchday. Here is a timeline that works for most away matches across Latin America.

Morning (6-8 hours before kickoff): Wake up, eat a solid breakfast. Do not skip this — you may not have another proper meal until after the match. Check your phone: confirm the kickoff time has not changed (it happens more often than you think). Charge your phone to 100%. Pack light: ticket (digital or physical), cash, ID or passport copy, small water bottle, sunscreen if the match is in the afternoon. Leave everything else at your accommodation.

Early afternoon (3-4 hours before kickoff): Head toward the stadium zone. This is when la previa starts in earnest. Find the visiting fans' gathering point. Eat, drink moderately, absorb the atmosphere. This is often the most memorable part of the entire day.

Pre-entry (90-120 minutes before kickoff): Begin making your way toward the visiting section entrance. Queues form early for big matches, and security checks can be slow. Have your ticket ready. Remove any prohibited items from your pockets (lighters, bottle caps, batteries, coins — lists vary by stadium). Be patient. The entry process in Latin American stadiums is often chaotic but it moves.

Inside the stadium (60 minutes before kickoff): Find your spot. In the visiting section, this is usually standing room with no assigned seats. Get there early enough to claim a good position. The atmosphere builds from here — the drums start, the flags go up, the chants begin. This is why you traveled.

The match: Sing, jump, suffer, celebrate. Ninety minutes of pure emotion. Stay aware of your surroundings, keep your phone secure, and hydrate if the heat is brutal.

Post-match: You will likely be held in the stadium for 15-45 minutes after the final whistle. Use this time to decompress, exchange contact details with new friends, and plan your exit. When released, follow the police corridor. Head directly to your transport or accommodation. Do not linger in unfamiliar areas after dark, especially after a heated match.

"The away-day does not end at the final whistle. It ends when you walk through your own front door, exhausted and already planning the next one."

The 10 Commandments of the Traveling Fan

After years of away-days across the continent, these are the non-negotiable rules that every traveling fan should live by. Tattoo them on your brain.

  1. Thou shalt plan thy trip but leave room for the unexpected. The best away-day stories come from the things you did not plan. Book the bus and the bed. Leave the rest to fate.
  2. Thou shalt buy tickets only from official sources. No exceptions. No "my friend has a contact." No Instagram resellers. Official channels or nothing.
  3. Thou shalt carry cash in the local currency. ATMs near stadiums are either empty, broken, or surrounded by a queue of a hundred fans. Withdraw in the city center, well before matchday.
  4. Thou shalt not wear thy colors in hostile territory. Your jersey goes on inside the stadium and comes off outside. Your scarf stays in your bag until you reach the visiting section. This is wisdom, not weakness.
  5. Thou shalt respect la previa. The pre-match is sacred. Eat, drink, sing, connect. But know your limits. A fan who is too drunk to remember the match is a fan who wasted the trip.
  6. Thou shalt travel in a pack. A lone visiting fan in an unfamiliar city is vulnerable. A group of three or four is a tribe. Find your people before you leave home.
  7. Thou shalt arrive early and leave last. Get to the stadium with time to spare. Stay after the match until the authorities release the visiting section. Patience is not optional.
  8. Thou shalt learn at least one chant. You do not need to know the entire songbook. But knowing one chant, singing it with full lungs alongside your fellow travelers, transforms you from a tourist into a hincha.
  9. Thou shalt respect the host city and its people. You are a guest. Enjoy the local food, learn a few words of the local dialect, thank the people who help you, and leave the city better than you found it. A good visiting fan is an ambassador.
  10. Thou shalt always plan the next one. An away-day is never a one-time event. It is the beginning of an addiction. The bus home is for recovering and for choosing the next destination.
