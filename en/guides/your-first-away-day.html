Your First Away-Day: The Complete Beginner's Guide

Everything you need to know to survive — and love — your very first away match in Latin America.

Updated Feb 2026 15 min read

Is This Your First Away-Day? Start Here

Welcome. The fact that you are reading this means you have already taken the first and most important step: deciding that watching football from your couch is no longer enough. You want to feel the roar of forty thousand voices shaking the concrete beneath your feet. You want to taste the smoke from the grills outside the stadium, sing songs you have not yet learned, and walk away with a story you will retell for the rest of your life.

An away-day — traveling to another city or country to watch a football match — is one of the most thrilling experiences the sport has to offer. But we will not lie to you: it can also be overwhelming if you go in unprepared. Different cities, unfamiliar stadiums, local customs, ticket logistics, and safety considerations can turn excitement into anxiety very quickly.

That is exactly why this guide exists. We have distilled years of away-day experience across Latin America into a single, practical, step-by-step resource built specifically for first-timers. No prior knowledge assumed. No gatekeeping. Just honest, friendly advice to help you have the best possible time.

Before you dive in

This guide focuses on domestic away-days in Latin American leagues — Argentina, Brazil, Colombia, Mexico, Chile, and beyond. If you are traveling internationally for a match, many of the same principles apply, but you will also want to check visa requirements and our Complete Away-Days Guide for cross-border specifics.

Choose an Easy Match to Start With

Not all matches are created equal, and your first away-day is not the time to go for the most intense derby on the continent. Think of it like learning to swim: you start in the shallow end.

Go Domestic First

Pick a match in the same country you are already visiting or living in. Crossing international borders adds complexity — customs, currency exchange, different phone networks — that you simply do not need on your first attempt. Save the Libertadores away-legs for when you have a few domestic trips under your belt.

Keep the Distance Short

Your first trip should ideally be somewhere you can reach in two to four hours by bus or a short domestic flight. A shorter journey means less can go wrong with transportation, and if something unexpected happens, you have an easier path home. In Argentina, that might mean Buenos Aires to La Plata. In Colombia, Bogotá to Tunja. In Brazil, São Paulo to Campinas.

Avoid High-Rivalry Matches

Clásicos and derbies are incredible — but they also come with heightened emotions, heavier police presence, more restricted access for visiting fans, and a higher chance of ticket scarcity. For your debut, pick a regular league fixture. The atmosphere at even a mid-table match in Latin America will blow your mind if you are used to watching from abroad.

Quick Decision Checklist

  • Distance from your base: Under 4 hours of travel each way
  • Rivalry level: Low to moderate (no clásicos)
  • Ticket availability: Check online — are tickets still on sale a week before? Good sign
  • Weekday or weekend: Weekend matches give you more flexibility with return travel
  • Stadium reputation: Look up the venue — some stadiums are naturally more welcoming to first-timers

The First-Timer's Kit

Packing for an away-day is an art. You want to be prepared for anything without lugging around a suitcase. Here is exactly what should go in your backpack.

The Essentials (Non-Negotiable)

  • Passport or national ID: Always carry valid identification. A photocopy stored separately from the original is a smart backup. Many stadiums in Latin America require ID to enter.
  • Match ticket (printed + digital): Print your ticket even if you have a digital version. Phone batteries die, screens crack, and stadium Wi-Fi is not something you should ever rely on.
  • Cash in local currency: Bring more than you think you will need. Vendors outside stadiums, street food stalls, and many transport options are cash-only. ATMs near stadiums are often out of service on matchday.
  • Phone with offline maps: Download the city map on Google Maps or Maps.me before you leave. You need to navigate without relying on mobile data.
  • Portable charger: A fully charged power bank is as important as your ticket. Your phone is your map, your translator, your camera, and your emergency communication tool.

Comfort and Safety

  • Small daypack: A compact backpack or crossbody bag that you can keep in front of you in crowds. Leave expensive backpacks at the hotel.
  • Water bottle: Staying hydrated sounds obvious until you have been standing in the sun for three hours. Note that some stadiums do not allow you to bring bottles inside — check the venue rules and be prepared to empty it before entry.
  • Sunscreen and hat: Latin American sun is no joke, especially in open-air stadiums with no shade. Apply before you arrive.
  • Rain jacket or poncho: Weather changes fast. A lightweight packable rain jacket takes up almost no space and can save your day.
  • Snacks: Energy bars, nuts, or fruit. If your bus is delayed or the lines outside the stadium are long, you will thank yourself.

Nice to Have

  • Team scarf or colors: Wearing your team's colors is part of the experience — but read the room. If you are attending as a neutral, consider wearing something plain and picking up a local scarf outside the stadium.
  • Earplugs: Latin American stadiums are incredibly loud. If you are sensitive to noise, a pair of earplugs lets you enjoy the atmosphere without ringing ears afterward.
  • Small first-aid basics: Band-aids, pain relievers, and anti-diarrheal medication. You probably will not need them. But if you do, you will be very glad you packed them.
  • Notebook or journal: There is something special about writing down your first matchday impressions in the moment. Your future self will appreciate it.

What NOT to bring

Leave valuables at your accommodation. No laptops, no expensive jewelry, no large amounts of cash. Do not bring anything you cannot afford to lose. Most stadiums prohibit large bags, glass bottles, umbrellas (yes, even umbrellas), and professional cameras. Check the specific stadium's prohibited items list before you go.

Step by Step: Before, During, and After

Here is your complete timeline, from the moment you decide to go until you are safely back home reliving every moment.

2-3 Weeks Before: Planning

  • Confirm the match date and time. Schedules change frequently in Latin American leagues. Check the official league website, not just third-party apps. TV broadcasting rights can shift kickoff times with little notice.
  • Buy your ticket. Use official club websites or authorized resellers only. Read our Tickets Without Scams guide for detailed instructions on avoiding fraud.
  • Book transportation. Bus, flight, or shared ride — book early for the best prices. For bus travel, companies like Busbud, RedBus, or local platforms let you compare routes and times.
  • Book accommodation if needed. If the match is an evening kickoff and the return journey is long, stay overnight. Look for lodging near the stadium or near the bus terminal for an early departure the next day.
  • Research the city and stadium. Read up on the neighborhood around the stadium. Know which areas to avoid, where to eat, and how to get from the transit hub to the ground.

The Day Before: Final Prep

  • Check your ticket status. Log in and make sure your digital ticket is accessible. Print a backup.
  • Charge all devices. Phone, power bank, camera — everything to 100%.
  • Download offline maps. Open Google Maps, search for the city, and download the area. This takes two minutes and could save your day.
  • Pack your bag. Use the kit list above. Lay everything out, check it twice, and pack light.
  • Share your itinerary. Send your travel plans, accommodation address, and match details to a friend or family member. This is not paranoia — it is common sense.
  • Get cash. Withdraw local currency from a bank ATM (not a street-corner exchange). Bring enough for transport, food, a drink, and a small emergency fund.

Matchday: The Big Day

  • Arrive at the city early. Give yourself at least three to four hours before kickoff to settle in, eat, and soak up the pre-match atmosphere. Rushing is the enemy of enjoyment.
  • Eat before heading to the stadium. Stadium food is part of the experience, but do not rely on it as your only meal. Eat something substantial beforehand — you will be standing and singing for hours.
  • Head to the stadium 90 minutes before kickoff. This gives you time to find your gate, clear security, locate your seat or standing section, and watch the stadium fill up.
  • Follow the crowd. When you exit the metro or bus, follow the stream of fans heading toward the stadium. They know the way, and there is safety in numbers.
  • Stay aware. Keep your phone in a front pocket or zipped bag. Do not flash expensive items. Be friendly but stay alert, especially in crowded areas around the turnstiles.
  • Soak it in. This is the moment. When the teams walk out and the stadium erupts, let yourself feel it. Sing along even if you do not know the words. Clap. Jump. This is why you came.

After the Match: Getting Home Safely

  • Do not rush the exit. Wait for the initial crush at the gates to ease. Leaving five minutes later can mean the difference between a stressful squeeze and a calm walk.
  • Follow the main flow. Stick with the majority of fans heading toward public transport. Avoid empty side streets, especially at night.
  • Have your return transport sorted. Know exactly which bus, metro line, or ride-share pickup point you need. Do not leave this to figure out in the post-match chaos.
  • Eat and decompress. Find a place near the terminal or your hotel to grab food and talk about the match. The post-match meal is one of the best parts of any away-day.
  • Document your experience. Write a few notes, save your ticket stub, post a photo. Your first away-day deserves to be remembered properly.

The 5 Most Common Rookie Mistakes

We have seen these mistakes dozens of times. Every single one is avoidable.

1. Buying Tickets from Scalpers or Unverified Sellers

This is the number one way first-timers ruin their day. You arrive at the stadium full of excitement, hand over your ticket, and the scanner flashes red. Counterfeit. The scalper is long gone, and so is your money. Always buy through official channels: the club website, authorized physical ticket offices, or verified platforms. If a deal sounds too good to be true, it absolutely is. Our Tickets Without Scams guide covers this in depth.

2. Wearing the Wrong Colors in the Wrong Neighborhood

Football identity runs deep in Latin America. Wearing a River Plate shirt while walking through a Boca-heavy neighborhood is not just a fashion choice — it can attract unwanted attention. If you are a neutral fan, wear plain clothes and buy a scarf once you are near the stadium. If you are supporting the away team, put your shirt on inside the stadium, not on the street.

3. Not Having a Plan B for Transportation

Buses get canceled. Flights get delayed. Ride-share apps surge to five times the normal price after a big match. Always have a backup plan. Know the address of your accommodation, have the number of a local taxi company saved in your phone, and keep enough cash for an emergency cab. The fans who get stranded are almost always the ones who assumed everything would go perfectly.

4. Arriving at the Stadium Too Late

Arriving ten minutes before kickoff might work at some European grounds, but not here. Security lines can be long and slow. Gates sometimes close temporarily when a section fills up. The pre-match atmosphere — the tifos, the songs building to a crescendo, the smoke and confetti — is half the experience. If you arrive late, you miss the best part. Aim for ninety minutes before kickoff. Minimum.

5. Draining Your Phone Battery Before Kickoff

You are in a new city, everything is exciting, and you are filming and photographing everything. By the time you reach the stadium, your phone is at twelve percent. Now you have no map, no ticket, and no way to call anyone. Bring a power bank. Use airplane mode when you do not need data. Save your battery for when it matters: getting in, enjoying the match, and getting home safely.

First-Timer FAQ

Is it safe to attend a football match alone in Latin America?

Yes, thousands of solo travelers attend matches safely every week. The key is preparation: stick to official ticket channels, avoid wearing the away team's colors in the wrong neighborhoods, arrive and leave with the crowd, and always let someone know your plans. For your very first match, consider joining a group or finding a travel companion through online football communities. Facebook groups, Reddit, and even Twitter are full of fans who are happy to welcome a newcomer. The overwhelming majority of matchday experiences are positive, joyful, and completely safe.

How far in advance should I plan my first away-day?

For a standard league match, two to three weeks is usually enough. For derbies, international matches, or knockout rounds, plan at least four to six weeks ahead. Tickets for high-demand fixtures can sell out within hours, and affordable accommodation disappears fast. Start by confirming the match date on the league's official website, since schedules often change due to TV broadcasting, weather, or security decisions. Once the date is locked, buy your ticket first, then transportation, then accommodation — in that order of priority.

Do I need to speak Spanish or Portuguese to get by on matchday?

Knowing a few basic phrases helps enormously and locals truly appreciate the effort. Learn greetings, how to ask for directions, numbers for negotiating prices, and key football vocabulary like "entrada" (ticket), "tribuna" (stand), "hinchada" (fan base), and "gol" (you know this one). That said, the football community is incredibly welcoming to foreign fans. A smile, a scarf, and genuine enthusiasm go a long way even with zero language skills. Download an offline translation app as a backup — Google Translate allows you to download entire language packs for offline use.

What if my team loses — is it dangerous to leave the stadium?

After any match, especially a heated one, follow the crowd and leave calmly with the majority of supporters heading in the same direction. Avoid celebrating or provoking if you are surrounded by the opposing team's fans. Police and security typically manage crowd flow after the final whistle, so follow their instructions. In many stadiums, away fans are held for 15 to 30 minutes after the match to allow the home crowd to disperse — this is normal and actually a safety measure designed to protect you. Use this time to chat with fellow fans, check your return transport schedule, and mentally replay the highlights. Losing stings, but it is part of the beautiful game — and it makes the next victory even sweeter.
