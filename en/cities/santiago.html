Santiago de Chile

Football beneath the Andes. Four iconic stadiums, Chile's fiercest derbies, a passionate fan culture built on decades of rivalry, and a city that makes every visiting fan feel the intensity. This is your complete guide.

Why Santiago?

Santiago de Chile is a city that does not immediately announce itself as a football capital. It does not have the sheer density of Buenos Aires or the mythological status of Rio de Janeiro. But underestimate Santiago at your own peril. Beneath the surface of this sprawling, modern metropolis — pressed between the Andes to the east and the coastal range to the west — lies a football culture of extraordinary passion, deep-rooted rivalries, and a fan identity that is among the most intense on the continent.

Three clubs dominate the city and, by extension, the entire country: Colo-Colo, the most popular club in Chile and the only Chilean team to win the Copa Libertadores; Universidad de Chile, the eternal rival, with a fanatical following that considers itself the soul of Chilean football; and Universidad Católica, the third force, based in the affluent eastern sector of Santiago, with a growing trophy cabinet and a loyal fanbase. A fourth club, Unión Española, adds historical depth and represents the legacy of Spanish immigrants in Chilean football. Together, these clubs create a triangular rivalry system that fuels the entire Chilean Primera División.

What makes Santiago special for the traveling fan is the combination of accessible infrastructure, affordable costs, and a football atmosphere that — while not as globally famous as its Argentine or Brazilian counterparts — delivers raw, authentic passion that has not been sanitized by over-commercialization. You can walk from a craft beer bar in Bellavista to the gates of Estadio Nacional in 20 minutes. You can ride the Metro from your hostel in Providencia directly to the Superclásico. Santiago is compact, navigable, and built for a football trip that does not require a logistics degree to plan.

"Santiago's football is an acquired taste. It does not scream at you from the airport. But once you feel the Garra Blanca roar at the Monumental or hear the Bulla shake the Nacional on a cold winter night, you understand. This city takes its football personally."

The Andes provide a dramatic backdrop that no other football city on the planet can match. Standing in the upper tier of San Carlos de Apoquindo on a clear autumn afternoon, watching Católica play with a wall of snow-capped mountains behind the goal, is one of those moments that reminds you why you became a traveling football fan in the first place. Santiago is not just a city to watch football — it is a city to feel it.

Must-Visit Stadiums

Santiago's four main stadiums offer distinctly different experiences — from the colossal Monumental to the intimate setting of Santa Laura. Each has its own character, its own crowd, and its own matchday ritual.

Estadio Monumental David Arellano — Colo-Colo

Capacity: ~47,000
Location: Macul, Av. Marathon 5300
Opened: 1989

The fortress of Colo-Colo and the largest club-owned stadium in Chile. The Monumental David Arellano — named after the club's legendary founder — is where the most popular team in the country plays its home matches. On big nights, particularly Superclásico fixtures and Copa Libertadores dates, the stadium fills to capacity with the Garra Blanca (Colo-Colo's main organized fan group) dominating the south end with banners, drums, smoke, and a wall of noise that hits you like a physical force. The atmosphere in the Galería Sur on a packed night is among the most intense in South American football.

The stadium is located in the Macul district, in the southeastern sector of the city. The surrounding area is primarily residential and lacks the bar-and-restaurant infrastructure you find near some Argentine or Brazilian stadiums. The pre-match scene takes place largely inside and immediately outside the ground, with street food vendors setting up along Av. Marathon hours before kickoff.

How to get there: Metro Line 4 (blue) to Estadio Monumental station — it is literally named after the ground. Exit the station and follow the crowd. On matchday, the streets are closed to traffic and the walk from Metro to turnstile takes about 10 minutes. This is one of the easiest stadium-to-metro connections in all of South American football.

HinchaViajero Tip

Arrive at the Monumental at least 90 minutes before kickoff to absorb the pre-match atmosphere. The buildup outside the south end — drums, chanting, flags being unfurled — is a spectacle in itself. If you want to experience the Garra Blanca terrace but prefer something slightly less intense, the Galería Norte is also lively but more manageable for a first-timer.

Estadio Nacional Julio Martínez Prádanos — Universidad de Chile

Capacity: ~48,000
Location: Ñuñoa, Av. Grecia 2001
Opened: 1938 (renovated multiple times)

The Estadio Nacional is not just a football venue — it is a monument to Chilean history. Built in 1938, it hosted the 1962 FIFA World Cup, serving as the stage where Chile achieved its greatest sporting moment (third place on home soil). But the stadium also carries a darker legacy: after the 1973 military coup, it was used as a detention and torture center by the Pinochet regime. Today, memorial plaques and a human rights exhibit inside the complex honor the victims. The Nacional is a place where sport and history are inseparable.

On the football pitch, the Estadio Nacional is the primary home of Universidad de Chile, the club known as "La U." The team's organized fan group, Los de Abajo, is one of the most famous barras bravas in South America. When they fill the Galería Sur of the Nacional, the noise and visual spectacle — choreographed tifos, torrents of confetti, pyrotechnics, and an uninterrupted songbook — create one of the most electrifying atmospheres you will find anywhere in Chile. A night match at the Nacional under floodlights, with Los de Abajo in full voice, is a bucket-list football experience.

The stadium sits in the Ñuñoa district, a middle-class residential area with good infrastructure and a comfortable feel. The Parque Estadio Nacional surrounding the complex offers green space for pre-match walks.

How to get there: Metro Line 5 (green) to Estadio Nacional station. Like the Monumental, the station is named for the ground and sits within a short walk of the entrances. Alternatively, numerous bus routes along Av. Grecia and Av. Irarrázaval serve the area.

Historical significance

If you visit the Estadio Nacional, take time to visit the memorial spaces and the human rights exhibit. The stadium's role during the 1973 coup is a fundamental part of Chilean history. Guided tours of the memorial area are available — check with the Instituto Nacional de Derechos Humanos for schedules. Understanding this history will deepen your appreciation of what the stadium means to the city.

San Carlos de Apoquindo — Universidad Católica

Capacity: ~14,000
Location: Las Condes, Camino Las Flores 13000
Opened: 1988

If there is one stadium in South America where the setting rivals the football, it is San Carlos de Apoquindo. Nestled in the foothills of the Andes in the wealthy Las Condes district, this compact ground offers views of the mountain range that look like they belong on a postcard. On a clear afternoon — especially in autumn and spring, when the Andes are capped with snow — the visual experience of watching football here is unmatched on the continent.

The stadium is the home of Universidad Católica, the "Cruzados." Católica has become the dominant force in Chilean football over the past decade, winning multiple league titles and building a reputation for smart, possession-based football. The fanbase, while smaller than Colo-Colo's or La U's, is passionate and loyal. The intimate size of San Carlos means the noise-to-capacity ratio is excellent — 14,000 fans here can sound like 30,000 elsewhere.

The downside of San Carlos is its location: it sits in the far eastern reaches of the city, at the base of the mountains, far from the Metro network. Getting there requires planning.

How to get there: There is no Metro station nearby. The most practical options are: taxi or ride-hailing app (Uber, DiDi, inDrive) from anywhere in Santiago (20-40 minutes depending on traffic), or bus routes that run along Av. Las Condes / Av. Apoquindo toward the eastern end. On matchday, there is often shuttle service organized by the club from specific gathering points — check Católica's social media channels before the match. Plan your return in advance: post-match taxi availability in this area can be limited.

HinchaViajero Tip

Try to attend a Sunday afternoon match at San Carlos de Apoquindo when the sky is clear. The combination of football, mountain views, and golden-hour sunlight is one of the most photogenic experiences in South American football. Bring a jacket — the foothills get cold quickly once the sun drops behind the peaks.

Estadio Santa Laura — Unión Española

Capacity: ~20,000
Location: Independencia, Av. Santa Laura 162
Opened: 1922

Santa Laura is the oldest surviving stadium in Chilean football and the home of Unión Española, the club founded by the Spanish immigrant community in 1897. If the Monumental and the Nacional represent the grand scale of Chilean football, Santa Laura represents its roots: neighborhood football, intimate crowds, and a sense of history that seeps from every brick.

Unión Española may not have the massive following of the "big three," but the club has a proud history — multiple league titles, a Copa Libertadores semi-final appearance (2011), and a community identity that transcends results. Attending a match at Santa Laura is a window into what Chilean club football was like before corporate sponsorship and all-seater regulations reshaped the landscape.

How to get there: Metro Line 3 (red) to Hospitales station, then a short 10-minute walk. Alternatively, bus routes along Av. Independencia drop you right at the stadium's doorstep. The area is safe and well-connected.

Derbies You Need to Experience

Santiago's football identity is defined by two great rivalries. These matches are not just sporting contests — they are cultural events that divide families, dominate conversations for weeks before and after, and define what it means to be a football fan in Chile.

Colo-Colo vs Universidad de Chile — The Superclásico

Hincha Index: 90/100

The biggest match in Chilean football and one of the most intense derbies in South America. Colo-Colo versus Universidad de Chile is not merely a rivalry — it is the central fault line of Chilean football identity. Every Chilean who cares about football has a side. There is no neutrality.

The narrative runs deep. Colo-Colo, founded in 1925, is the most popular club in the country — the "people's team," with a fanbase that stretches from Arica to Punta Arenas. Universidad de Chile, founded in 1927, carries the identity of the university, the intellectual tradition, and a romantic self-image as the club that suffers more but loves harder. Colo-Colo fans call La U fans "llorones" (crybabies). La U fans call Colo-Colo fans "indios" (a reference to the club's indigenous Mapuche-inspired name and crest). The insults have been traded for a century.

On the pitch, the Superclásico is almost always chaotic, emotional, and unpredictable. Red cards, last-minute goals, controversial referee decisions, and post-match arguments are the norm, not the exception. When the match takes place at the Monumental, the Garra Blanca turns it into a cauldron. When it takes place at the Nacional, Los de Abajo respond with equal ferocity. Either way, the atmosphere is South American football at its rawest.

Superclásico safety

The Superclásico is a high-security event. Police presence is massive, surrounding streets are closed, and tensions run extremely high. As a foreign visitor, buy a home-section ticket and blend in with the home fans. Do not wear any colors associated with the away team. Arrive early, leave promptly after the final whistle using Metro or a pre-booked ride-hailing app. Do not linger in the streets. Visiting fan sections are sometimes banned entirely for this fixture — check ANFP security announcements beforehand.

Universidad de Chile vs Universidad Católica — Clásico Universitario

Hincha Index: 75/100

The "University Derby" is the second great rivalry of Santiago. While it does not carry the same explosive intensity as the Superclásico, the Clásico Universitario has its own distinct flavor — rooted in class, geography, and institutional identity.

Universidad de Chile represents the public university tradition — secular, democratic, historically linked to progressive politics. Universidad Católica represents the Catholic university tradition — conservative, affluent, associated with the wealthier eastern districts of Santiago (Las Condes, Vitacura, Lo Barnechea). The match is, in many ways, a reflection of Chile's social divide: east versus west, private versus public, tradition versus rebellion.

On the pitch, the Clásico Universitario has produced some of the finest tactical battles in Chilean football. Católica's methodical, European-influenced style against La U's more emotional, high-intensity approach creates a fascinating contrast. The atmosphere, while more controlled than the Superclásico, is still electric — especially when the match is played at the Nacional with La U as hosts.

Other Santiago Rivalries

Beyond the two flagship derbies, Santiago offers additional rivalry matches worth knowing about:

  • Colo-Colo vs Universidad Católica: The third leg of the triangle. Increasingly competitive as Católica has risen to challenge Colo-Colo's historic dominance. Less socially charged than the Superclásico, but tactically intense.
  • Unión Española vs any of the big three: When Unión Española plays at Santa Laura against Colo-Colo, La U, or Católica, the intimate setting amplifies the underdog energy. These fixtures have a David-and-Goliath quality that makes them compelling.
  • Copa Chile clashes: The Chilean domestic cup often produces surprise fixtures and heightened emotions, particularly in knockout rounds. These matches can be played at neutral venues, adding a different atmosphere to the experience.

Where to Stay

Santiago is a sprawling city, but the areas that matter for the traveling football fan are concentrated in a central-east corridor that is well connected by Metro. Here are the best neighborhoods to base yourself.

Providencia

Best for: The all-round base camp. Providencia is Santiago's sweet spot — central, safe, well-connected, and packed with restaurants, cafes, and nightlife. It sits right on Metro Line 1, giving you direct access to transfer stations for Lines 4 (Monumental) and Line 5 (Nacional). The area around Av. Providencia and Manuel Montt is full of accommodation options from hostels to mid-range hotels. Walking along Av. Providencia on any given evening, you will find bars screening live football. This is where most savvy visitors base themselves, and for good reason.

Bellavista

Best for: Nightlife and culture. Bellavista is Santiago's bohemian quarter — street art, live music, craft beer bars, and the legendary Patio Bellavista commercial complex. It sits between Providencia and the Cerro San Cristóbal, making it walkable to both. On matchday evenings, the bars here fill with fans watching games on screens and debating results over pisco sours. If you want a pre-match or post-match atmosphere that extends beyond the stadium, Bellavista delivers. Hostels are slightly cheaper than Providencia. Safety: generally fine on the main streets, but exercise caution on quieter side streets late at night.

Lastarria / Barrio Italia

Best for: The cultural traveler who also loves football. Lastarria is Santiago's most aesthetically charming neighborhood — cobblestone streets, independent bookshops, art galleries, and intimate cafes. Barrio Italia, a few blocks east, has a similar vibe with design shops and craft breweries. Both neighborhoods are close to Metro Line 1 and within easy reach of the stadiums. Accommodation here skews toward boutique hotels and upscale Airbnbs, but the area's walkability and charm make it worth considering if your budget allows.

Ñuñoa

Best for: Proximity to the Estadio Nacional. Ñuñoa is the residential district where the Estadio Nacional sits. It has a local, neighborhood feel — less touristy than Providencia or Bellavista, but with its own excellent restaurants, cafes (especially around Plaza Ñuñoa), and a strong community identity. If you are primarily in Santiago to watch La U at the Nacional, staying in Ñuñoa puts you within walking distance. Metro Line 6 and Line 3 serve the area. Accommodation is affordable, and the pre-match walk from your hostel to the stadium through residential streets is a quintessentially local experience.

Macul

Best for: Proximity to Estadio Monumental. Macul is the working-class district east of Ñuñoa where the Monumental sits. It is less polished than Providencia and has fewer tourist-oriented services, but it is authentic and gives you direct access to Colo-Colo's home ground. Metro Line 4 runs through the district. If you are visiting specifically for a Colo-Colo match and want the full neighborhood experience, Macul is an option. Just note that accommodation choices are more limited here — many visitors prefer to stay in Providencia and Metro over on matchday.

HinchaViajero Tip

For a first visit to Santiago, stay in Providencia. It gives you the best balance of safety, nightlife, food, and stadium access. Metro Line 1 from Providencia connects to the transfer points for Lines 4 and 5, meaning both the Monumental and the Nacional are 30-40 minutes away by Metro. That is hard to beat.

Getting Around

Santiago has the best public transport system in South America. The Metro is clean, fast, affordable, and — crucially — connects directly to the two biggest stadiums in the city. Here is everything you need to know.

The bip! Card

Santiago's equivalent of a transit card. You need a bip! card to ride the Metro and public buses. Buy one at any Metro station for approximately 1,500 CLP (about 2 USD) and load credit onto it at station machines or convenience stores. One Metro ride costs approximately 800 CLP (~1 USD) during peak hours and slightly less off-peak. The card works on all Red buses (the city's bus network) and the Metro. It is the first thing you should acquire upon arriving in Santiago.

Metro de Santiago

Seven lines covering the central and suburban areas of the city. Runs from approximately 5:30 AM to 11:00 PM (slightly later on Fridays, slightly earlier on Sundays). The key lines for the traveling fan:

  • Line 1 (red): The main east-west line. Runs through Providencia, the city center, and connects to transfer stations for Lines 4 and 5. Your lifeline.
  • Line 4 (blue): Connects to Estadio Monumental station (Colo-Colo). Transfer from Line 1 at Tobalaba or Vicente Valdés.
  • Line 5 (green): Connects to Estadio Nacional station (U de Chile). Transfer from Line 1 at Baquedano.
  • Line 3 (red/brown): Useful for reaching Independencia (near Santa Laura, Unión Española).
  • Line 6 (purple): Useful for Ñuñoa and connecting to other lines.

On matchdays, expect the Metro to be extremely crowded in the direction of the stadium for about 2 hours before kickoff, and in the opposite direction for about 1 hour after the final whistle. Plan accordingly — arrive early, and consider riding one or two stops past the stadium station and walking back to avoid the worst crush.

Red Buses

Santiago's public bus network covers the entire city and runs from early morning until late at night. Buses are identified by a letter-number code (e.g., 506, D09). Google Maps and the Transantiago app provide real-time routing and bus tracking. Buses are paid with the bip! card — no cash accepted. For reaching San Carlos de Apoquindo (Católica), where there is no Metro, buses along the Av. Apoquindo corridor are the main public transport option.

Taxis and Ride-Hailing Apps

Uber and DiDi both operate in Santiago and are widely used. inDrive is another popular option where you negotiate the fare. Traditional black-and-yellow taxis are abundant but always use the meter or agree on a price before boarding. For returning from stadiums at night, ride-hailing apps are your best bet — but expect surge pricing and 5-10 minute waits after big matches. Pre-book or have your app ready with a pickup point slightly away from the main crowd exits.

From the Airport

Aeropuerto Internacional Arturo Merino Benítez (SCL) is located approximately 25 km northwest of the city center. Options for reaching your accommodation:

  • Centropuerto / TurBus: Direct airport bus to the Pajaritos Metro station (Line 1), costing around 2,000 CLP (~2.50 USD). From Pajaritos, ride Line 1 to Providencia or the city center. This is the cheapest option.
  • Official taxi (Transvip / Transfer Delfos): Shared or private transfer to your accommodation, 15,000-25,000 CLP (~18-30 USD) for a private ride.
  • Uber / DiDi: Available at the airport, typically 12,000-20,000 CLP (~15-25 USD) to Providencia. Follow signs to the ride-hailing pickup zone.

Food & The Previa

The pre-match culture in Santiago is less street-centric than in Buenos Aires but has its own distinct flavor — a mix of street food, bar culture, and the communal ritual of gathering before heading to the ground.

The Completo

Chile's national street food and the closest thing to a universal pre-match snack. A completo is a hot dog loaded with mashed avocado, chopped tomato, mayonnaise, and sauerkraut. The "italiano" version (avocado, tomato, mayo — the colors of the Italian flag) is the most popular variant. You will find completo stands near every stadium on matchday, and at dedicated "completerías" across the city. Cost: 2,000-3,500 CLP (~2.50-4 USD). It is messy, it is absurdly large, and it is essential to the Santiago matchday experience.

Choripán

Just as in Argentina, the choripán (grilled chorizo sausage in bread with pebre — the Chilean version of chimichurri) is a matchday staple. Street vendors set up outside the Monumental and the Nacional hours before kickoff, and the smell of the grill is your compass toward the stadium. Chilean choripán tends to be slightly smaller than the Argentine version but packed with flavor. Pair it with a cold Cristal or Escudo beer from a street vendor for the full pre-match ritual. Budget: 3,000-5,000 CLP (~4-6 USD) for choripán and beer.

The Terremoto

Chile's most famous (and dangerous) cocktail — and a pre-match rite of passage for the brave. A terremoto ("earthquake") is a tall glass of pipeño (young, sweet, slightly fermented white wine), pineapple ice cream, and a splash of fernet or grenadine. It tastes deceptively sweet and innocent, but the sugar-alcohol combination hits hard. The traditional place to have a terremoto is La Piojera, a legendary dive bar in the city center near Mercado Central, where the drink was supposedly invented. One terremoto before the match is a fun tradition. Two is a mistake. Three and you will not remember the match.

Alcohol at stadiums

Alcohol is not sold inside Chilean football stadiums. This is a longstanding rule enforced at all Primera División venues. Have your pre-match drinks before you enter the ground. Do not try to sneak alcohol in — bag checks at turnstiles are thorough, and you risk being denied entry.

Pre-Match Bars

Santiago does not have the same concentration of matchday bars directly adjacent to stadiums that you find in Buenos Aires. The pre-match bar culture is more dispersed:

  • For Colo-Colo matches (Monumental): The main pre-match gathering is outside the stadium itself. For a sit-down bar experience, try the establishments along Av. Macul or closer to Metro Macul before heading to the ground.
  • For La U matches (Nacional): The area around Plaza Ñuñoa has several bars and restaurants that fill with blue-shirted fans before home matches. Walk from Plaza Ñuñoa to the stadium (15-20 minutes) through the residential streets — the atmosphere builds as you get closer.
  • For neutral watching: Bellavista bars screen every major Chilean football match. Flannery's (Irish pub in Providencia), Bar Liguria (Providencia), and the bars around Patio Bellavista are all reliable options for watching a match if you do not have a ticket.

Other Food Worth Trying

  • Empanadas de pino: Chile's version of the empanada — filled with minced beef, onion, hard-boiled egg, olives, and raisins. Best from traditional bakeries or market stalls. One empanada is a snack; two is a meal.
  • Sopaipillas: Deep-fried pumpkin dough, sold on street corners across the city. Eaten plain or with pebre (salsa) or mustard. A rainy-day staple — and perfect for the colder months of the Chilean league season.
  • Mote con huesillo: A traditional cold drink made from husked wheat and dried peaches in sweet syrup. Sold from street carts. Refreshing on warm matchday afternoons.
  • Cerveza artesanal: Santiago's craft beer scene has exploded. Barrio Italia and Bellavista have multiple brewpubs worth visiting before or after a match.

Money-saving tip

Chilean pesos are the only currency you need. ATMs ("cajeros automáticos") are abundant and most accept international cards. Credit cards are widely accepted in restaurants, shops, and supermarkets. For the absolute best exchange rate, use a no-fee international debit card (Wise, Revolut) and withdraw from Scotiabank or Banco Estado ATMs, which tend to have the lowest foreign card fees. Street money changers exist in the city center but are unnecessary — ATMs give better rates with less risk.

Safety Tips

Santiago is one of the safest major cities in South America, but standard precautions apply — especially in the football context, where large crowds, alcohol, and tribal emotions create situations that require awareness.

  • Do not wear rival colors near the stadium. This is the golden rule in Chilean football, just as it is everywhere in South America. If you are going to a Colo-Colo match, do not wear a U de Chile jersey. If you do not have home team colors, wear neutral clothing. Inside the stadium, blending in with the home fans is the safest approach for a visitor.
  • Stay out of the galerías unless you know what you are getting into. The standing terrace sections (galerías) in Chilean stadiums are where the barras bravas congregate. The atmosphere is incredible but the environment is intense — pushing, jumping, pyrotechnics, and zero personal space. If it is your first Chilean match, stick to the seated tribuna sections. You will still experience an amazing atmosphere with more room to breathe.
  • Phone theft is the biggest risk. Santiago's most common crime against tourists is phone snatching, especially near Metro stations and crowded areas. Keep your phone in your front pocket or an inside jacket pocket. On matchday, be especially vigilant in the crush around Metro exits near stadiums.
  • Use ride-hailing apps after night matches. The Metro closes around 11:00 PM. If a match ends late, do not assume the Metro will still be running for your return trip. Have Uber, DiDi, or inDrive installed and ready. Request the car from a location slightly away from the stadium exits where traffic is less congested.
  • Avoid walking through unfamiliar neighborhoods at night. Santiago's stadium districts (Macul, parts of Independencia) are safe during daylight and on matchday when police are present, but they are not neighborhoods to explore on foot late at night. Head back to your accommodation promptly after the match.
  • Watch out for post-match street tensions. After derbies and high-stakes matches, tensions can spill onto the surrounding streets. Carabineros (Chilean police) use water cannons and tear gas more liberally than police in most European countries. If you see or smell tear gas, move upwind immediately. Do not engage with any groups that appear confrontational. Walk away quickly and calmly.
  • Carry a copy of your passport. Leave the original at your accommodation. To enter most Chilean stadiums you need an ID document — a photocopy of your passport or your actual passport. Check the specific requirements on the club's website before matchday.
  • Altitude and smog are not issues. Unlike some other South American football cities, Santiago sits at approximately 520 meters above sea level — no altitude concerns. However, Santiago does have smog issues during winter months (June-August). If you have respiratory sensitivities, carry any necessary medication.
About visiting fan sections

Chilean football has a complicated history with visiting fans. Depending on the fixture, the ANFP (Asociación Nacional de Fútbol Profesional) may allow or ban visiting supporters on a match-by-match basis. For high-risk fixtures like the Superclásico, visiting fans are typically banned. For lower-risk matches, a visiting section ("galería visitante") may be available. Always check the official ANFP and club social media accounts in the days before the match to confirm whether visiting fans are permitted and which sections are allocated.

Santiago is moderately affordable for football tourists. Match tickets for regular league games cost 15-40 USD. A hostel bed in Providencia or Bellavista runs 12-25 USD per night. Street food and pre-match staples (completo, choripán, beer) cost 5-12 USD for a full meal. A Metro bip! card costs about 2 USD and daily rides add up to 3-5 USD. A budget matchday — including transport, food, ticket, and accommodation — comes to roughly 45-80 USD. For a comfortable mid-range day with a hotel and restaurant meals, expect 100-180 USD.
