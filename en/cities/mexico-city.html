Why Mexico City?

Mexico City is not just the capital of Mexico — it is the capital of Mexican football. With over 22 million people in its metropolitan area, CDMX is the third largest city in the world and one of the most vibrant metropolises on the continent. But what makes it essential for the traveling fan goes far beyond its size.

This is home to Estadio Azteca, the largest football temple in Latin America and one of only two stadiums in the world to have hosted two World Cup finals. Here, three first-division clubs coexist — America, Cruz Azul and Pumas UNAM — with rivalries that bring the entire city to a standstill. This is where the Clasico Nacional was born, the most-watched match on the continent.

But CDMX is not just football. It is a city with a food scene that rivals the best in the world: tacos al pastor at 2 AM, markets bursting with flavor, tlacoyos on the street and craft mezcal bars. It is a city accessible by air from virtually anywhere in Latin America, with a metro system that takes you to every stadium for less than half a dollar. It is a city that welcomes you with open arms, feeds you like there is no tomorrow, and pulls you into its organized chaos until you feel like you belong.

If you have never been to see football in CDMX, you are missing a fundamental experience. And if you have been, you know there is always a reason to come back.

Must-Visit Stadiums

Estadio Azteca (Club America)

Capacity: 87,523 | Location: Coapa, Coyoacan

The Colossus of Santa Ursula. The cathedral of Mexican football. Estadio Azteca is not just a stadium — it is a monument. Opened in 1966, it has hosted two World Cup finals (1970 and 1986), including the match where Maradona scored the Goal of the Century. It is the home of Club America, Mexico's most successful club, and when it fills for a Clasico Nacional, the energy is absolutely indescribable.

How to get there: Metro Line 2 to Tasquena, then Tren Ligero (light rail) to Estadio Azteca station. You can also get there from Coyoacan by Uber or DiDi for around $50-80 MXN. Metrobus Line 1 drops you in the southern zone with a quick connection.

HinchaViajero Tip

Arrive at least 2 hours before kickoff. The Azteca parking area transforms into a massive food fair with taco stands, tortas, esquites and beer vendors. The pre-match scene outside the Azteca is an experience in itself. Plus, entry queues can be long, especially for derbies.

Estadio Ciudad de los Deportes (Cruz Azul)

Capacity: 34,000 | Location: Benito Juarez

Cruz Azul's home ground. A more intimate stadium than the Azteca, where the proximity to the pitch creates an electric atmosphere. La Maquina's fanbase fills the ground with a passion you can feel in every corner, and night matches under the lights have a special charm.

How to get there: Metro Line 7 to San Antonio station. The stadium is just a few blocks away. The area is well connected with plenty of food options nearby. Uber and DiDi work perfectly in this zone.

Estadio Olimpico Universitario (Pumas UNAM)

Capacity: 63,186 | Location: Ciudad Universitaria, Coyoacan

A stadium with history and art. The Olimpico de CU hosted the 1968 Olympic Games and sits within the UNAM campus, a UNESCO World Heritage Site. The Diego Rivera mural on the facade is iconic. Pumas' fanbase — La Rebel — gives the stadium a university-bred, counter-cultural character that sets it apart from any other venue in Mexico.

How to get there: Metro Line 3 to Universidad station, then the free Pumabus shuttle within the campus. Metrobus Line 1 also stops at CU. The campus is huge, so plan extra time to walk to the stadium.

Where to Stay

Roma / Condesa

The best overall choice for the traveling fan. Trendy, safe neighborhoods with a spectacular food scene. Specialty coffee shops, chef-driven restaurants, late-night taquerias and sports bars line tree-shaded streets with art deco architecture. Perfectly connected by Metro (Line 1, Insurgentes and Chapultepec stations) and Metrobus (Line 1 along Insurgentes). From here you can reach any stadium in 30-50 minutes.

Centro Historico (Historic Center)

The budget-friendly option with history. Hostels from $150 MXN a night, walkable, with the Zocalo and colonial buildings as a backdrop. The area has plenty of life during the day and direct transport everywhere (Metro Line 2, Zocalo station). Note: it can be noisy at night and some side streets are not the safest after 11 PM.

Coyoacan

If you are heading to the Azteca or CU, Coyoacan is your neighborhood. Quieter than Roma, with a bohemian vibe inherited from Frida Kahlo and Diego Rivera. Excellent markets, garden cafes and a village-like atmosphere within the megalopolis. Close to the Tren Ligero that takes you to the Azteca and a stone's throw from Ciudad Universitaria.

Polanco

The premium option. An upscale, very safe neighborhood with international hotels, high-end restaurants and the Soumaya Museum. More expensive than the other options, but if budget is not a concern, Polanco offers total comfort. Metro Line 7 connects you to Estadio Ciudad de los Deportes.

Areas to Avoid

Tepito and Doctores are not recommended for tourists, especially at night. Although they are relatively close to the Historic Center, the safety situation is different. If your GPS routes you through these areas, change your route. Always use ride-hailing apps in these zones.

Getting Around

CDMX has a surprisingly good and ridiculously cheap public transport system. The Metro is your best friend for getting around the city and reaching the stadiums.

Rush Hour Warning

Avoid the Metro between 7-9 AM and 5-7 PM. It is absolutely packed, to the point where boarding can be a struggle. If you need to travel during those hours, use Uber/DiDi or the Metrobus which is slightly less crowded. Outside of rush hour, the Metro is comfortable, fast and safe.

The Pre-Match Scene in CDMX

The pre-match experience in Mexico City is an extension of the street culture that defines the city. Nobody arrives at the stadium ten minutes before kickoff — in CDMX, the match starts hours earlier and food is the star of the show.

Outside the Azteca: The parking area and surrounding streets of the Colossus of Santa Ursula transform into a massive food market hours before the match. Taco al pastor stands with the spit turning, esquites with chili and lime, milanesa tortas the size of your head, grilled elotes and draft beer at laughable prices. This pre-match scene is a culinary experience that justifies the trip even if you don't have a ticket.

Roma / Condesa: If you want a more relaxed pre-match vibe or you are waiting for a match at another stadium, Avenida Alvaro Obregon and its cross streets are full of sports bars with big screens, craft beer and good vibes. A safe area with an international atmosphere and options for every taste.

What you have to eat:

  • Tacos al pastor: The signature dish of CDMX. Adobo-marinated pork carved from the spit with pineapple. Look for stands with a queue — if there is a queue, it is good.
  • Tlacoyos: Corn masa stuffed with beans, requesón cheese or chicharrón. Pre-Hispanic street food sold at markets and on the street.
  • Esquites: Corn kernels in a cup with mayonnaise, chili, lime and cheese. The perfect pre-match snack.
  • Tortas: Mexican sandwiches that could feed an entire family. The milanesa torta is an engineering marvel of the food world.

Beer: Draft beer in CDMX is absurdly cheap — $20-40 MXN ($1-2 USD) at many stands and popular bars. National brands like Victoria, Modelo and Pacifico are the most common. If you prefer craft beer, Roma and Condesa have an excellent craft scene.

Safety Tips

CDMX has a reputation as a dangerous city, but the reality for tourists and traveling fans who use common sense is much friendlier than the headlines suggest. Tourist and football areas are generally safe, and millions of people move through the city every day without incident.

  • Use Uber, DiDi or Cabify at night instead of street taxis. Street cabs in CDMX are not always safe, especially those without an app or visible meter. Ride-hailing apps are cheap and reliable.
  • Keep your phone secure on the Metro. Pickpockets operate at busy stations and in crowded carriages. Keep your phone in your front pocket and wear your backpack on your chest.
  • Don't flash expensive items. Pricey watches, chains, expensive cameras — leave all of that at your accommodation. Go out with the essentials: phone, cash for the day, ID.
  • Stadium areas are well-policed on matchday. Police deploy major operations for Liga MX matches, especially derbies. Follow the officers' instructions and stay out of trouble.
  • The altitude hits hard. CDMX sits at 2,240 meters (7,350 feet) above sea level. If you are coming from a coastal city, you will feel it: shortness of breath, headaches, fatigue. Hydrate heavily, avoid excessive alcohol on your first day and don't push yourself physically in the first 24 hours. Most people adjust within 1-2 days.
  • Earthquake zone. CDMX is in a seismic zone. If you feel a tremor, stay calm, move away from windows and objects that could fall, and follow local guidance. Modern buildings are designed to withstand earthquakes.
About the altitude

If you arrive in CDMX on matchday itself, take it easy. Drink water constantly, eat light and don't sprint to your seat. The altitude (2,240m / 7,350ft) hits especially hard for those coming from sea-level cities. If possible, arrive a day early to acclimatize.
