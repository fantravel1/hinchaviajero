Overview — The Most Iconic Stadium in South America

There is no stadium in South America that carries more weight than the Maracana. Its formal name is Estadio Jornalista Mario Filho, but the world knows it simply as Maracana — a name derived from the Tupi-Guarani word for a species of macaw that once inhabited the banks of the river flowing near the site. Since it opened its gates on June 16, 1950, the Maracana has been the stage for some of the most dramatic, beautiful, and heartbreaking moments in the history of football. It is not merely a stadium. It is the physical embodiment of Brazil's relationship with the sport it gave a soul to.

Sitting in the Tijuca district of Rio de Janeiro, the Maracana dominates the landscape with a curved, modernist silhouette that has become as recognizable as Sugarloaf Mountain or Christ the Redeemer. At its current capacity of approximately 78,000, it is the largest stadium in Brazil and one of the largest in South America. But numbers alone do not explain the Maracana. What makes it singular is the density of history soaked into its concrete — two World Cup finals, the Maracanazo, Pele's thousandth goal, Zico's legend, decades of Fla-Flu derbies, Copa Libertadores nights under floodlights, and an Olympic ceremony. No other stadium on the continent has accumulated such a layered, complex, emotionally charged heritage.

For the traveling football fan, the Maracana is the ultimate pilgrimage. Whether you attend a midweek Brasileirao fixture with 30,000 Flamengo fans filling the lower tiers, or you witness a sold-out Fla-Flu derby with 78,000 people producing a wall of noise driven by samba drums, the experience is transformative. The stadium is accessible, affordable, and — thanks to Metro Line 2 — remarkably easy to reach from any part of Rio. You do not need to be a fan of Brazilian football to appreciate the Maracana. You just need to love the game.

"The Maracana is not a place where you watch football. It is a place where football watches you — and decides whether you are worthy of understanding what the beautiful game truly means."

History — From the 1950 World Cup to the Present

Construction and the 1950 World Cup

The Maracana was built specifically to host the 1950 FIFA World Cup, the first tournament held after World War II. Brazil, eager to announce itself to the post-war world as a modern, ambitious nation, committed to constructing the largest stadium on Earth. Designed by architects Rafael Galvao and Pedro Paulo Bernardes Bastos with structural engineering by Antonio Alves de Noronha, the stadium was a marvel of reinforced concrete — a sweeping, oval bowl that could hold close to 200,000 spectators in its original configuration. Construction began in August 1948 and was completed in a frantic rush just days before the tournament kicked off in June 1950.

The 1950 World Cup was played in a unique final-round-robin format rather than a knockout final. But fate arranged the schedule so that the decisive match — Brazil versus Uruguay on July 16, 1950 — was effectively a final. Brazil needed only a draw to win their first World Cup. The entire nation expected victory. Newspapers had already printed editions declaring Brazil as champions. An estimated 199,854 spectators packed into the Maracana, a number that remains the largest recorded attendance for a football match in history.

The Maracanazo

What happened next became one of the defining moments in sporting history. Brazil took the lead through Friaca in the 47th minute, and the Maracana erupted. But Uruguay equalized through Juan Alberto Schiaffino in the 66th minute, and then Alcides Ghiggia scored the winner in the 79th minute. The silence that descended upon 200,000 people is described by those who were there as the most devastating sound — or absence of sound — they had ever experienced. Grown men wept openly. The Brazilian goalkeeper, Moacir Barbosa, was blamed for the defeat and lived the rest of his life as a scapegoat, shunned by a nation that could not forgive what had happened inside its new cathedral.

The Maracanazo — the "blow of the Maracana" — scarred Brazilian football so deeply that its effects lasted decades. Brazil did not wear white jerseys again after 1950, switching to the now-iconic yellow and green. The trauma of that afternoon shaped the Brazilian approach to the game: the obsessive pursuit of beauty, of joy, of a style so dominant that defeat would become impossible. When Brazil finally won the World Cup in 1958 and again in 1962, it was seen as exorcism — but the ghost of the Maracanazo has never fully left the building.

The Golden Era: Pele, Zico, and Brazilian Legends

Throughout the second half of the twentieth century, the Maracana served as the stage for the greatest players in Brazilian football history. Pele scored his thousandth career goal at the Maracana on November 19, 1969, against Vasco da Gama — a moment broadcast live to the nation and celebrated as a national holiday. Zico, the "White Pele," made the Maracana his personal arena during his legendary career with Flamengo in the 1970s and 1980s, scoring hundreds of goals and leading the club to its first Copa Libertadores title in 1981. Garrincha dazzled crowds with his impossible dribbling. Romario and Ronaldo followed, each adding chapters to the stadium's mythology.

The Maracana also hosted legendary international fixtures: Copa America finals, Copa Libertadores deciders, and qualifier matches for every World Cup from 1954 onward. By the 1990s, despite its aging infrastructure and declining capacity due to safety concerns, the Maracana remained the symbolic heart of Brazilian football — the one venue that every player, every fan, and every opponent measured themselves against.

Renovation: 2010-2013

In preparation for the 2014 FIFA World Cup and the 2016 Rio Olympics, the Maracana underwent a comprehensive reconstruction between 2010 and 2013. The iconic two-tier bowl was replaced with a modern, single-tier design featuring all-seater sections, a new roof structure covering all seats, improved accessibility, modern media facilities, and a capacity reduced to approximately 78,838. The renovation cost approximately 500 million USD and was completed in April 2013.

The 2014 World Cup final — Germany 1-0 Argentina in extra time — was played at the Maracana, as was the 2016 Olympic opening and closing ceremony. While some purists mourned the loss of the original standing terraces and the raw, chaotic atmosphere they produced, the renovated Maracana is a world-class modern stadium that still generates an atmosphere that few venues on the planet can match when it is full.

Historical context

The original Maracana at its peak held close to 200,000 people. To put that in perspective, the current largest stadium in the world — Rungrado May Day Stadium in Pyongyang — holds 114,000. The 1950 Maracana was a venue on a scale that has never been replicated and almost certainly never will be.

Capacity & Seating Sections

The current Maracana holds approximately 78,000 spectators in an all-seater configuration. The stadium is divided into several distinct sections, each with different price points, atmospheres, and experiences for the matchday visitor.

Lower Tier (Arquibancada Inferior)

The lower tier wraps around the entire pitch at a steep angle that brings fans remarkably close to the action. The sections behind each goal — known as the "Geral Norte" and "Geral Sul" — are where the torcidas organizadas (organized fan groups) position themselves. This is the epicenter of the atmosphere: the drums, the massive flags, the coordinated chanting, the jumping, and the pyrotechnics (when they manage to sneak them in). If you want the full Maracana experience, this is where you need to be. Tickets for these sections are also the cheapest, typically 15-25 USD for regular matches.

Upper Tier (Arquibancada Superior)

The upper tier offers a panoramic view of the entire pitch and the surrounding cityscape. The atmosphere is more relaxed than the lower tier, with families, older fans, and tourists mixing with regular supporters. Sightlines are excellent from virtually every seat. This is the best option if you want to appreciate the tactical shape of the match while still being immersed in the crowd noise rising from below. Prices typically range from 20-35 USD.

West Side (Tribuna de Honra)

The western side of the stadium contains the Tribuna de Honra — the VIP and hospitality sections, the press box, and the best lateral views of the pitch. Ticket prices here can reach 40-50 USD for big matches. The atmosphere is subdued compared to the ends, but the viewing angle is superb.

Visitor Section (Setor Visitante)

Away fans are allocated a dedicated section, usually in one corner of the upper tier. Access is through a separate entrance with its own security screening. In high-risk matches, away fans are held inside the stadium for 30-60 minutes after the final whistle to allow the home crowd to disperse. If you are attending as a neutral, avoid the visitor section unless you are genuinely supporting the away team — police enforce strict separation.

Seating advice

For your first visit, the lower tier behind the home goal is the most immersive experience — you will be surrounded by the torcida organizada in full voice. If you prefer a calmer experience with better sightlines, choose the upper tier on the sideline. Never sit in a section associated with one club while wearing the colors of their rival.

Getting There — Metro Line 2

Getting to the Maracana is one of the simplest stadium commutes in South American football, thanks to Metro Line 2. The dedicated Maracana station is located directly adjacent to the stadium complex. On matchdays, you step off the train and you are essentially at the gates. No long walks through unfamiliar neighborhoods, no confusing bus routes, no expensive taxis stuck in traffic. The metro is fast, cheap, safe, and the overwhelmingly recommended way to travel.

From Copacabana or Ipanema (Zona Sul)

Take Metro Line 1 from any station in the Zona Sul (General Osorio, Cantagalo, Siqueira Campos, Cardeal Arcoverde, Botafogo) and ride to Estacio station. Transfer to Line 2 and continue two stops to Maracana station. Total journey time: approximately 30 minutes. Cost: under 2 USD with a rechargeable MetroRio card, which you can purchase at any station.

From Centro (Downtown)

If you are in Rio's Centro district, take Line 1 or Line 2 depending on your starting station. Most Centro stations are on Line 1 — ride to Estacio and transfer to Line 2. Journey time: approximately 15-20 minutes.

From Lapa

Lapa does not have a metro station directly, but Cinelandia and Gloria stations on Line 1 are within walking distance. From either, ride to Estacio and transfer to Line 2. Total time: about 25 minutes.

Matchday Metro Service

On matchdays, MetroRio extends operating hours and increases train frequency on Line 2 to handle the matchday crowds. Trains typically run until at least one hour after the final whistle. Stations near the stadium can become extremely crowded immediately before and after the match. To avoid the worst of the crush, arrive at the stadium at least 90 minutes before kickoff and wait 20-30 minutes after the final whistle before heading to the station.

Alternatives: Uber, 99, and Taxis

Ride-hailing apps work in Rio, but using them on matchday to reach the Maracana is strongly discouraged. Traffic around the stadium is gridlocked for hours before and after the match, and surge pricing can be extreme. If the metro has stopped running after a late-finishing match, Uber or 99 are your best options for getting back to your accommodation — but walk a few blocks away from the stadium before requesting a ride to escape the worst of the congestion and the surge zone.

HinchaViajero Tip

Buy your MetroRio rechargeable card before matchday and load it with enough credit for the return journey. On matchday, the queues at ticket machines inside Maracana station can be long after the match. Having a pre-loaded card lets you walk straight through the turnstiles.

Flamengo & Fluminense Torcidas

The Maracana is the shared home of two of the most passionate fanbases in Brazilian football. Understanding the torcidas that fill this stadium is essential to understanding the matchday experience.

Flamengo — The Nacao Rubro-Negra

Flamengo is the most supported football club in Brazil, with an estimated 40 million fans nationwide. On matchday at the Maracana, the "Red-and-Black Nation" transforms the stadium into a cauldron of noise, color, and controlled chaos. Flamengo's principal torcidas organizadas — including Torcida Jovem do Flamengo, Urubu, and Raca Rubro-Negra — occupy the lower tiers behind the goal and produce an atmosphere that is physically overwhelming. The bateria (drum section) plays non-stop from before kickoff until after the final whistle: samba rhythms, call-and-response chants, and original compositions that the entire stadium follows.

When Flamengo plays at home, particularly in Brasileirao night matches or Copa Libertadores fixtures, the Maracana regularly fills to 60,000-70,000. For high-profile matches — the Fla-Flu, the Classico dos Milhoes against Vasco, or a Libertadores semifinal — the stadium sells out completely, and the noise reaches a level that makes conversation impossible. Massive red-and-black flags blanket entire sections. Confetti rains down at kickoff. The crowd bounces in unison, producing a visual rhythm that matches the samba beat from the drums. It is, simply, one of the greatest football atmospheres on the planet.

Fluminense — The Tricolor Carioca

Fluminense's fanbase is smaller than Flamengo's in raw numbers, but the devotion is no less intense. The "Tricolor" — named for the club's burgundy, green, and white colors — brings a different energy to the Maracana: more melodic, more emotional, with an undercurrent of aristocratic pride that reflects the club's origins as Rio's elite social institution. Fluminense's torcidas organizadas, particularly Bravo 52 and Young Flu, occupy their designated sections and produce a passionate, musical atmosphere driven by coordinated singing rather than sheer volume.

When Fluminense plays at home, the attendance is generally lower than Flamengo — typically 20,000-40,000 for regular matches — but the intensity per person can be just as high. For the Fla-Flu derby or a Copa Libertadores night, the Tricolor torcida fills their end and matches Flamengo's noise with a fierce pride that borders on defiance. Fluminense fans see themselves as the cultured, loyal alternative to Flamengo's mass popularity, and that identity fuels an atmosphere that is concentrated, emotional, and deeply authentic.

The Fla-Flu Derby

When Flamengo and Fluminense meet at the Maracana, the stadium splits in two. One end turns red and black; the other turns tricolor. The noise is deafening before the teams even enter the pitch. The Fla-Flu is the most famous derby in Brazilian football — the name itself has become a generic term in Portuguese for any fierce two-sided contest. The 1963 Fla-Flu drew an official attendance of 177,656, a world record for a club match. Every meeting since has carried that weight of history. Attending a Fla-Flu at a full Maracana is one of the top-five fan experiences in world football.

HinchaViajero Tip

If you attend a Fla-Flu, choose which side you want to be on before buying your ticket. The sections are strictly separated. Wearing Flamengo colors in the Fluminense section (or vice versa) is not just socially unacceptable — stadium security will deny entry. If you are a neutral tourist, pick the Flamengo end for sheer volume or the Fluminense end for a more musical, underdog atmosphere.

Tickets — Prices, Platforms & Advice

Getting tickets for the Maracana is straightforward for most matches, though high-demand fixtures require advance planning. Here is everything you need to know.

Where to Buy

  • Official club websites and apps: The most reliable method. Both Flamengo and Fluminense sell tickets through their official platforms. For Flamengo matches, check the club's website or the "Nacao" app. For Fluminense, check the club's site or the "Flu-ID" platform. Some tickets are reserved for club members (socios), but general public tickets are available for most regular-season matches.
  • Futebol Card / Ingresso Rapido: Third-party platforms authorized by the clubs and the CBF (Brazilian Football Confederation) to sell tickets. These are legitimate and reliable.
  • Maracana box office: For regular Brasileirao matches, tickets are often available at the stadium box office on matchday. Arrive early — the box office opens several hours before kickoff. Cash and cards are accepted.

What You Need to Know

  • ID is mandatory: Brazilian stadiums require identification for entry. Bring your passport or a clear photocopy. The ticket is linked to your ID number.
  • Digital tickets: Most tickets are now digital — you will receive a QR code on your phone or email. Print a backup copy in case your phone battery dies.
  • Buy derbies in advance: Fla-Flu derbies, Classico dos Milhoes matches, and Copa Libertadores knockout rounds sell out. Buy these tickets as soon as they go on sale, typically 1-2 weeks before the match.
  • Avoid street resellers: Do not buy tickets from scalpers outside the stadium. Counterfeit tickets are common, and digital ticketing systems make physical tickets nearly obsolete. If the deal seems too good to be true, it is.
Scam alert

Around the Maracana on matchday, you will encounter people offering tickets at inflated prices or "guaranteed entry." These are almost always scams. The digital ticketing system means that a physical piece of paper with a barcode printed on it is worthless unless it matches a valid digital record linked to your ID. Buy only through official channels.

Tijuca Food Scene & Pre-Match Eating

The matchday food experience at the Maracana begins long before the gates open. The streets surrounding the stadium — particularly the pedestrian routes from Maracana metro station — transform into an open-air food market 2-3 hours before kickoff. The smell of charcoal smoke, the sizzle of meat on portable grills, and the sound of bottle caps popping off ice-cold beers create a sensory atmosphere that is the perfect appetizer for the match itself.

Street Food Near the Stadium

  • Pasteis: Deep-fried pastry pockets — the quintessential Brazilian street food. Fillings include queijo (cheese), carne (ground beef), camarao (shrimp), and palmito (palm heart). Crispy on the outside, steaming on the inside. A pastel and a cold beer is the classic Maracana pre-match combo. Cost: 1-3 USD each.
  • Churrasquinho: Small skewers of grilled meat sold from portable charcoal grills. Chicken, beef, and the legendary coracao de frango (chicken hearts) — try them at least once, they are surprisingly delicious. Cost: 2-4 USD per skewer.
  • Acai: A thick, frozen acai berry bowl, usually topped with granola and sliced banana. Cold, energizing, and available from vendors on virtually every corner near the stadium. Perfect fuel for hours of jumping and chanting. Cost: 3-5 USD.
  • Cachorro-quente: Brazilian hot dogs, topped with an absurd variety of condiments including corn, peas, shoestring potatoes, mayonnaise, ketchup, and mustard. A gloriously excessive pre-match meal. Cost: 2-4 USD.
  • Beer: Ice-cold Brahma, Skol, or Antarctica — the holy trinity of Brazilian mass-market beer. Served in cans or small cups from every conceivable vendor. On a hot Rio evening, nothing beats a cold cerveja while the pre-match atmosphere builds around you. Cost: 2-3 USD.

Sit-Down Meals in Tijuca

For a proper pre-match meal, the Tijuca neighborhood offers traditional botecos (neighborhood bars) and restaurants serving authentic carioca cuisine. A few options worth seeking out:

  • Feijoada: Brazil's national dish — a rich, slow-cooked stew of black beans with various cuts of pork, served with white rice, farofa (toasted cassava flour), orange slices, and couve (sauteed collard greens). Traditionally eaten on Saturdays. If your match falls on a Saturday, find a boteco in Tijuca that serves feijoada and you have the perfect pre-match ritual. Cost: 8-15 USD.
  • Prato feito (PF): The Brazilian "set plate" — rice, black beans, a protein (grilled chicken, steak, or fish), salad, and farofa. Cheap, filling, and available at any boteco or lanchonete in Tijuca. Cost: 5-8 USD.
  • Galeto: Roasted chicken, typically served with farofa and vinagrete (a tomato and onion salsa). Several galeto restaurants operate near the Maracana and are popular with fans before and after matches.

Inside the Stadium

The renovated Maracana has food and beverage stands throughout the concourses. Options are limited compared to the street food outside — expect basic items like hot dogs, popcorn, soft drinks, and beer (when alcohol sales are permitted, which varies by state regulation and match classification). Prices inside the stadium are higher than outside. Eating before you enter is recommended.

HinchaViajero Tip

Arrive at the Maracana area 2-3 hours before kickoff. Eat on the street, soak in the atmosphere, then enter the stadium about 60-90 minutes before the match starts. The pre-match scene outside the Maracana is itself a spectacle — drums playing, fans singing, food sizzling — and rushing through it to get inside early means missing half the experience.

Safety — What Every Visiting Fan Needs to Know

The Maracana is generally safe on matchdays, but Rio de Janeiro requires more awareness than most cities. The stadium area benefits from heavy police presence before, during, and after matches, and the direct metro connection means you can travel to and from the ground without walking through unfamiliar neighborhoods. Follow these rules and you will have a safe, memorable experience.

  • Take the metro. Always. Metro Line 2 to Maracana station is the safest, fastest, and cheapest way to reach the stadium. Do not drive, do not take a taxi (traffic is gridlocked), and do not walk through the surrounding neighborhoods after dark. The metro corridor from the station to the stadium is policed and well-lit on matchdays.
  • Do not wear opposing team colors in the wrong section. This rule is non-negotiable. Wearing Fluminense colors in a Flamengo section (or vice versa) can provoke confrontations. If you are a neutral tourist, wear black, white, or the home team's colors. Stadium security checks for this at the gates.
  • Keep your phone hidden. Phone theft is the most common crime in Rio. Do not walk with your phone in your hand near the stadium, on the metro platform, or on the street. Keep it in a zipped inner pocket. Consider bringing a cheap backup phone and leaving your expensive one at your accommodation.
  • Avoid flashy accessories. Leave watches, jewelry, cameras with visible straps, and expensive headphones at your accommodation. Dress simply. A visibly wealthy tourist near a stadium is a target.
  • Carry cash in small bills. Street food vendors are cash-only. Bring R$10 and R$20 bills. Do not pull out a large wad of cash in public. Keep your matchday budget in one pocket and leave the rest at your accommodation.
  • Stay with the crowd after the match. After the final whistle, join the flow of people heading toward the metro station. Do not wander off the main routes, do not linger in the stadium surroundings after the crowd has dispersed, and do not explore side streets after dark.
  • Arrive early, leave promptly. Get to the stadium area at least 90 minutes before kickoff. Security screening can be thorough and queues build quickly. After the match, head straight to your transport. Waiting 15-20 minutes for the initial crush at the metro to subside is smart; waiting an hour in a deserted plaza is not.
  • Bring a passport copy. ID is required for entry. A clear photocopy or a photo on your phone usually works. Leave the original passport in your hotel safe.
Neighborhoods near the Maracana

The Maracana is located in the Tijuca district, which is a middle-class residential area that is generally safe. However, several nearby neighborhoods and favela communities are not tourist-friendly, especially after dark. Do not take shortcuts, do not follow strangers offering "faster routes," and do not explore side streets at night. Stick to the well-lit, policed corridor between the metro station and the stadium. After night matches, take the metro or a ride-hailing app directly to your accommodation.

High-risk match protocol

For derbies and other high-risk matches, Brazilian police implement a "protocolo de seguranca" that includes separated entry routes for home and away fans, staggered departure times, and an extended police cordon around the stadium. Follow police instructions at all times. If you are told to wait, wait. If you are directed to a specific exit, use it. These protocols exist because of a real history of post-match violence between rival torcidas.

Maracana Museum & Stadium Tour

Even if you cannot attend a live match, the Maracana offers a guided tour and museum experience that is one of the best in South American football. Standing on the pitch where Pele scored his thousandth goal, where Uruguay broke Brazil's heart in 1950, and where Germany lifted the 2014 World Cup is worth every centavo of the admission price.

What the Tour Includes

  • The Pitch: You walk onto the actual playing surface and can stand in the center circle, touch the grass, and take photos with the stands towering around you. On a quiet, empty-stadium day, the scale of the Maracana is even more impressive than during a match.
  • The Stands: Access to several sections of the seating bowl, including the areas where the torcidas organizadas stand during matches. The view from the lower tier behind the goal — imagining 20,000 fans packed in there drumming and jumping — is powerful.
  • The Locker Rooms: See the home and away dressing rooms where Pele, Zico, Ronaldo, Messi, Ronaldinho, and hundreds of other legends prepared for matches. The rooms are modern after the 2013 renovation, but the historical weight is palpable.
  • The Press Room: Where post-match press conferences are held. You can sit in the chair where managers and players face the media after historic victories and devastating defeats.
  • The Hall of Fame: The museum section contains memorabilia from across the Maracana's 75+ year history. Match balls from World Cup finals, jerseys worn by Brazilian legends, photographs from the 1950 construction, models of the original 200,000-capacity design, and interactive displays covering the major moments in the stadium's story. The section on the 1950 Maracanazo is particularly moving — the photographs of the stunned crowd, the newspaper headlines that had been pre-printed celebrating a Brazilian victory, and the personal stories of those who were there.
  • The Walk of Fame: A series of footprints in concrete from Brazilian football legends — similar to Hollywood's Walk of Fame but for the beautiful game. Find Pele, Garrincha, Zico, Romario, Ronaldo, and dozens more.

Practical Details

  • Hours: Daily, 9:00 AM to 4:00 PM on non-matchdays. The tour does not operate on matchdays or the day before certain high-profile matches (when the pitch is being prepared).
  • Cost: Approximately 15-20 USD per person. Children and seniors receive discounts.
  • Duration: Approximately 40-60 minutes for the guided tour.
  • Language: Tours are available in Portuguese and English. Audio guides are available in additional languages.
  • Booking: Reserve online in advance through the official Maracana tour website. Walk-up tickets are available but the tour can sell out on busy days, especially during Brazilian school holidays and international tourist season (December-February).
  • How to get there: Same as matchday — Metro Line 2 to Maracana station.
HinchaViajero Tip

If you have time for both the tour and a live match, do the tour first. Walking the empty stadium, learning the history, standing on the pitch in silence — it gives you a context that makes the matchday experience exponentially more powerful. When those 78,000 people start singing in the place you stood alone that morning, the emotional impact is extraordinary.

These estimates do not include accommodation — hostel beds in Copacabana run 12-25 USD per night, while hotels range from 40-120 USD.
